ABSTRACT. We describe complex conjugation on the primitive middle-dimensional algebraic de Rham cohomology of a smooth projective hypersurface defined over a number field that admits a real embedding. We use Griffiths’ description of the cohomology in terms of a Jacobian ring. The resulting description is algebraic up to transcendental factors explicitly given by certain periods.

JEEHOON PARK, JUNYEONG PARK, AND PHILSANG YOO

Let V be a smooth projective variety over a number field k. For each embedding σ : k ↪ C, we consider the associated complex manifold V_σ := (V ×_{k,σ} C)(C) and its Betti (singular) cohomology

H^i_B(V_σ, Q) = H^i((V ×_{k,σ} C)(C), Q).

After tensoring with C, we have the Hodge decomposition

P : H^i_B(V_σ, Q) ⊗_Q C ≅ ⨁_{p+q=i} H^{p,q}

where H^{p,q} stands for the Dolbeault cohomology group of V_σ. Here and elsewhere, we always identify Betti cohomology and analytic de Rham cohomology after tensoring with C via the de Rham theorem.

Now we note that if σ is a real embedding, then the complex conjugation c ∈ Gal(C/R) acts on V_σ which induces an involution ι_σ on H^i_B(V_σ, Q). On the other hand, clearly 1 ⊗ c is also an involution on H^i_B(V_σ, Q) ⊗_Q C. It is well-known that both involutions ι_σ ⊗ 1 and 1 ⊗ c switch the Hodge components H^{p,q} and H^{q,p} under P. In particular, 1 ⊗ c corresponds to the complex conjugation ˉ• on ⨁_{p+q=i} H^{p,q}, which makes (H^i_B(V_σ, Q) ⊗_Q C)^{1⊗c} ≅ H^i_B(V_σ, Q) ⊗_Q R ≅ H^i_B(V_σ, R) into a pure R-Hodge structure of weight i.

Example 1.1. Let E be an elliptic curve defined over Q with the canonical embedding σ : Q ↪ R ⊂ C. Then one can think of an element of H^1_B(E_σ, Q) ≅ H^1(C/Z^2, Q) as of the form Qdx + Qdy, where z = x + iy is a coordinate for C. Because σ is a real embedding, the map ι_σ satisfies ι_σ(dx) = dx and ι_σ(dy) = –dy. For the Hodge decomposition, we have

H^1_B(E_σ, Q) ⊗ C ≅ H^{1,0} ⊕ H^{0,1} ≅ Cdz ⊕ Cdˉz,

where dz = dx + idy and dˉz = dx – idy. Then both ι_σ ⊗ 1 and 1 ⊗ c clearly swap H^{1,0} and H^{0,1}, because (ι_σ ⊗ 1)(dx ± idy) = dx ∓ idy and (1 ⊗ c)(dx ± idy) = dx ∓ idy.

Now we give a brief review of Griffiths’ theory ([4]) and its refined version over \( k \) ([5]). We introduce a new variable \( y \) and consider the polynomial ring \( A \) and the polynomial \( S(y,x) \in A \) as follows:

\[
A := k[y, x], \quad S(y, x) := yG(x).
\]

We also introduce two new gradings, which we call charge $ch$ and weight $wt$:

$$
ch(y) := -e, \quad ch(x_i) := \deg(x_i) = 1,\quad wt(y)=1,\quad wt(x_i)=0
$$

for $i=0, \cdots, n$. For each $m \in \mathbb{Z}$, we denote the $ch\ m$ (respectively, $wt\ m$) homogeneous component by $A_m$ (respectively, $A_{(m)}$).

For each $i = 0, \cdots, n$, let us set $G_i(x) := \frac{\partial G(x)}{\partial x_i}$. Note that the smoothness of $X_G$ implies that $G_0, \cdots, G_n$ is a regular sequence. Let $Jac(S)$ be the Jacobian ideal in $A$, i.e., the homogeneous ideal of $A$ generated by $y G_0 = \frac{\partial S}{\partial x_0}, \cdots, y G_n = \frac{\partial S}{\partial x_n}$, and $G = \frac{\partial S}{\partial y}$. Define the background charge as follows:

$$
c_X := e-(n+1).
$$

Under these notations, P. Griffiths proved the following theorem.

## Theorem 2.1. [4] There is an isomorphism

$$
\phi_C : \frac{A_{c_X} \otimes_{k,\sigma} \mathbb{C}}{Jac(S) \cap (A_{c_X} \otimes_{k,\sigma} \mathbb{C})} \stackrel{\sim}{\longrightarrow} H_0 = \bigoplus_{p+q=n-1} H_0^{p,q}
$$

which sends the $wt\ r$ homogeneous part $A_{c_X,(r)} \otimes_{k,\sigma} \mathbb{C}$ (for $r \leq n-1$)$^1$ to $H_0^{n-1-r, r}$.

Let us briefly describe $\phi_C$. Consider the relative long exact sequence for the pair $(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathbb{P}^n \setminus X)$
$$
\cdots \longrightarrow H_{n-1}(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathbb{P}^n \setminus X) \longrightarrow H_n(\mathbb{P}^n \setminus X) \longrightarrow H_n(\mathbb{P}^n) \longrightarrow H_n(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathbb{P}^n \setminus X) \longrightarrow \cdots
$$

As we have an isomorphism $H_{n-1}(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathbb{P}^n \setminus X) \cong H_{n-1}(X)$ using a tubular neighborhood of $X$ in $\mathbb{P}^n$, it induces a tubular neighborhood map
$$
T: H_{n-1}(X, \mathbb{C}) \to H_n(\mathbb{P}^n \setminus X, \mathbb{C})
$$

On the other hand, the residue map
$$
res: H^n(\mathbb{P}^n \setminus X, \mathbb{C}) \to H^{n-1}(X, \mathbb{C})
$$

is locally defined by sending $\omega = \frac{dG}{G} \wedge \omega_1 + \omega_2$, where $\omega_1, \omega_2$ are holomorphic, to $\omega_1$, so it can be shown to respect the Hodge filtration and induces

$$
res: F^p H^n(\mathbb{P}^n \setminus X, \mathbb{C}) \to F^{p-1} H^{n-1}(X, \mathbb{C}).
$$

These two maps form the following commutative diagram

\[
\begin{array}{ccc}
H_{n-1}(X, \mathbb{C}) & \xrightarrow{\otimes} & H^{n-1}(X, \mathbb{C}) \\
\downarrow T && \uparrow res \\
H_n(\mathbb{P}^n \setminus X, \mathbb{C}) & \xrightarrow{\otimes} & H^n(\mathbb{P}^n \setminus X, \mathbb{C}) \\
&& \searrow res \\
&& \mathbb{C}
\end{array}
\]

in that

$$
\int_{[\gamma]} res([\omega]) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{-1}} \int_{T([\gamma])} [\omega]
$$

holds for any $[\gamma] \in H_{n-1}(X, \mathbb{C})$ and $[\omega] \in H^n(\mathbb{P}^n \setminus X, \mathbb{C})$.

Let

$$
\Omega_x = \sum_{i=0}^n (-1)^i x_i dx_0 \wedge \cdots \wedge \widehat{dx_i} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx_n
$$

Then the Griffiths map $\phi_C$ is given by

$$
\phi_C([y^i f(x)]) = res \left( \left[ \frac{(-1)^i i! f(x) \Omega_x}{G(x)^{i+1}} \right] \right)
$$

where $\left[ \frac{(-1)^i i! f(x) \Omega_x}{G(x)^{i+1}} \right]$ represents the cohomology class in $H^n(\mathbb{P}^n \setminus X, \mathbb{C})$.

## 2.2. The main result.

Let \(\mu\) be the \(\mathbb{C}\)-dimension of \(H^{n-1}_{\mathrm{prim}}(X, \mathbb{C})\). We fix an index set \(I\) of cardinality \(\mu\) and decompose

\[ I = I_0 \sqcup \cdots \sqcup I_{n-1} \]

such that the cardinality \(|I_q|\) of \(I_q\) is the \(\mathbb{C}\)-dimension of \(H_0^{n-q-1, q}\). It is well-known (see, for example, [3, 5.3]) that the diffeomorphism type of such a hypersurface \(X\) is determined by the degree \(e\) and the dimension \(n-1\), and indeed that

\[
\mu = \frac{e-1}{e} \left( (e-1)^n + (-1)^{n+1} \right)
\]

Note that if \(e = 1\), then indeed \(H^{n-1}_{\mathrm{prim}}(X, \mathbb{C}) = 0\) holds and everything vacuously works out. Henceforth, we assume \(e > 1\) and accordingly \(\mu > 0\).

For simplicity of presentation, we first assume \(n = 2m+2\) (\(m \geq 0\)) is even. In this case, the intersection pairing \(H_{2m+1}(X, \mathbb{Z}) \otimes H_{2m+1}(X, \mathbb{Z}) \to \mathbb{Z}\) is skew-symmetric and non-degenerate. Thus \(\mu\) is an even number \(2g\) for some positive integer \(g\). We choose a canonical basis \(\{\gamma_1, \cdots, \gamma_{2g}\}\) of \(H_{2m+1}(X, \mathbb{Z})\) (in the sense of [4, page 478]), that is, a \(\mathbb{Z}\)-basis of \(H_{2m+1}(X, \mathbb{Z})\) such that the intersection numbers \(\gamma_i \cdot \gamma_j\) give an integral skew-symmetric \(2g \times 2g\) matrix

\[
Q := \begin{pmatrix}
0 & \mathrm{id}_g \\
-\mathrm{id}_g & 0
\end{pmatrix},\quad \mathrm{id}_g = \text{the } g \times g \text{ identity matrix}
\]

On the other hand, given \(I = I_0 \sqcup \cdots \sqcup I_{2m+1}\), Hodge symmetry implies that the cardinality of \(I_0 \sqcup \cdots \sqcup I_m\) is \(g\). Then by (2.5) we can choose \(g\) elements of the form \(y^i f_{j_i}(x) \in A_c^X\), where \(i = 0, \cdots, m\) and \(j_i = 1, \cdots, |I_i|\), such that they are \(k\)-linearly independent modulo \(\mathrm{Jac}(S) \cap A_c^X\). Note that one has to have

\[
\deg f_{j_i} = c_X + i e = e - (n+1) + i e = e(i+1) - (n+1)
\]

We write these basis elements as

\[
\{U_1, U_2, \cdots, U_g\} := \{y^i f_{j_i} \mid i = 0, \cdots, m,\, j_i = 1, \cdots, |I_i|\}
\]

Having so chosen bases for

\[
\begin{array}{ccc}
H_{2m+1}(X, \mathbb{Z}) & \text{and} & \bigoplus\limits_{i=0}^{m} A_c^X,(i) / (\mathrm{Jac}(S)\cap A_c^X,(i))
\end{array}
\]

we form the \(g \times 2g\) period matrix

\[
P_X = \begin{pmatrix}
