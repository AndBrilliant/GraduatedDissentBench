ABSTRACT. J. Silverman proved that a dynamical system on 𝑃¹ descends to the field of moduli if it is polynomial or it has even degree, but for non-polynomial ones of odd degree the picture is less clear. We give a complete characterization of which dynamical systems over 𝑃¹ descend to the field of moduli.

We work over a perfect field 𝑘 with algebraic closure 𝐾.

Consider 𝜙 : 𝑃¹_𝐾 → 𝑃¹_𝐾 a finite map of degree 𝑑 ≥ 2. The interplay between the number-theoretic and dynamical properties of 𝜙 has been the subject of a lot of research in the last decades, see e.g. [Sil07].

The dynamics of 𝜙 do not change if we conjugate it by a projective linear transformation 𝑓 ∈ PGL₂(𝐾). Define 𝜙^𝑓 ≝ 𝑓⁻¹ ∘ 𝜙 ∘ 𝑓; this gives an action of PGL₂(𝐾) on the set of rational functions. A dynamical system ξ on 𝑃¹_𝐾 is an equivalence class for this action.

The Galois group Gal(𝐾/𝑘) acts on the set of dynamical systems by acting on the coefficients of the corresponding rational functions in 𝐾(𝑡). The field of moduli 𝑘_ξ is the subfield of 𝐾 fixed by the elements of Gal(𝐾/𝑘) mapping ξ to itself. If ξ descends to a dynamical system on a subextension 𝑘' ⊂ 𝐾, then clearly 𝑘_ξ ⊂ 𝑘'. It is then natural to ask whether ξ descends to a dynamical system on 𝑘_ξ.

Recall that the stabilizer 𝔄_𝜙 ⊂ PGL₂(𝐾) of 𝜙 is the subgroup {𝑓 ∈ PGL₂(𝐾) | 𝜙^𝑓 = 𝜙}; its isomorphism class only depends on ξ, so that 𝔄_ξ is well defined as an abstract group, and it is finite [Sil95, Proposition 4.1]. J. Silverman proved the following.

Theorem 1 ([Sil95, Theorem 5.1]). Let ξ be a dynamical system on 𝑃¹_𝐾, assume that char 𝑘 does not divide |𝔄_ξ|. If deg ξ is even or if ξ contains a polynomial, then ξ descends to a dynamical system on 𝑃¹_{𝑘_ξ}.

Furthermore, Silverman proved that ξ descends to a Brauer–Severi curve if the stabilizer 𝔄_ξ is trivial [Sil95, Theorem 2.1]. We prove that every dynamical system with non-trivial stabilizer descends to 𝑃¹_{𝑘_ξ}. This is somewhat counter-intuitive, since the case of non-trivial stabilizer is more complex.

We thus get a complete characterization of descent properties for dynamical systems on 𝑃¹_𝐾.

Theorem 2. Every dynamical system ξ = [𝜙] on 𝑃¹_𝐾 descends to a dynamical system on some Brauer–Severi curve defined over the field of moduli 𝑘_ξ. Furthermore, the following are equivalent.

R. Hidalgo proved that, in characteristic 0, every dynamical system \( \xi \) descends to a dynamical system on \( \mathbb{P}^1_{k'} \), where \( k'/k_\xi \) is an extension of degree at most 2 [Hid14]. As a consequence of Theorem 2, Hidalgo's theorem holds in any characteristic, since every dynamical system descends to a Brauer–Severi curve over \( k_\xi \) and every Brauer–Severi curve is split by an extension of degree at most 2.

Theorem 2 implies the following generalization of Silverman's result.

**Theorem 3.** *A dynamical system* \( \xi = [\varphi] \) *on* \( \mathbb{P}^1_K \) *descends to a dynamical system on* \( \mathbb{P}^1_{k_\xi} \) *if any of the following conditions holds.*

- The stabilizer \( A_\xi \) is non-trivial.
- The degree \( d \) is even.
- There exists an integer \( e \geq 2 \) such that the number of points with ramification index \( e \) is odd.

If \( \xi \) contains a polynomial, either \( \xi \) contains \( x^d \) (which is clearly defined over the field of moduli) or there is a unique \( K \)-point with ramification index \( d \), so that \( \xi \) satisfies the third condition of Theorem 3.

We mention that analogous problems for dynamical systems on \( \mathbb{P}^n \) are studied in [HM14, DS19, Breb]. Furthermore, recently F. Veneziano and S. Vishkautsan have studied similar problems for iterates of a dynamical system on \( \mathbb{P}^1 \) [VV24].

**A remark about Silverman's results.** Silverman does not assume the base field to be perfect, so our results seem to be less general in this regard. However, if we drop this hypothesis there is a technical flaw in Silverman's paper. Let \( k \) be an arbitrary field, \( \overline{K} \) an algebraic closure, \( K \subset \overline{K} \) the separable closure, \( k^p \subset \overline{K} \) the perfect closure. There is a natural identification of Galois groups \( \mathrm{Gal}(K/k) = \mathrm{Gal}(\overline{K}/k^p) \), call them \( G_k \).

In [Sil95, Proposition 1.2], it is stated that \( H^1(G_k, \mathrm{PGL}_2(\overline{K})) \) classifies Brauer–Severi curves over \( k \). However, the proof uses a reference [Sil09, §X, Theorem 2.2] which assumes the base field to be perfect. In general, Brauer–Severi curves over \( k \) are classified by \( H^1(G_k, \mathrm{PGL}_2(K)) \) [GS17, Theorem 5.2.1], while \( H^1(G_k, \mathrm{PGL}_2(\overline{K})) \) classifies Brauer–Severi curves over \( k^p \).

If \( \mathrm{char}\, k \neq 2 \), the natural map

\[
H^1(G_k, \mathrm{PGL}_2(K)) = \operatorname{Br}(k)[2] \to H^1(G_k, \mathrm{PGL}_2(\overline{K})) = \operatorname{Br}(k^p)[2]
\]

is an isomorphism, since \( \operatorname{Br}(k) \to \operatorname{Br}(k^p) \) is surjective [Jac96, Theorem 4.1.5] with char \( k \)-primary kernel. Still, Silverman's article remains flawed even if we assume \( \mathrm{char}\, k \neq 2 \). In fact, in the proof of [Sil95, Theorem 2.1(b)], the morphism \( \Phi = j g \varphi g^{-1} j^{-1} \) is defined over \( \overline{K} \) (because \( g \) is defined over \( \overline{k} \)) as opposed to \( K \): in order to verify that \( \Phi \) is defined over \( k \), Silverman checks that \( \Phi \) is Galois-invariant, but since \( \Phi \) is a priori defined over \( \overline{K} \) this only proves that \( \Phi \) is defined over \( k^p \).

Finally, let us mention that there is a way to study the problem even when the base field is not perfect. This is done by replacing Galois cohomology either with fppf cohomology or stack theory, such as in [BV24]. We don't know whether all the various results remain completely true in this generality. Still, most of their content remains true, see §4.

An element of 𝒩_φ induces an homomorphism Γ(𝒪_{ℙ¹_K}) = K → Γ(𝒪_{ℙ¹_K}) = K, hence we get a short exact sequence

1 → 𝒜_φ → 𝒩_φ → Gal(K/k)

Lemma 4. The image of 𝒩_φ → Gal(K/k) is Gal(K/k_ξ), hence we have a short exact sequence

1 → 𝒜_φ → 𝒩_φ → Gal(K/k_ξ) → 1.

Proof. Consider φ ∈ K(t) as a rational function. An element σ ∈ Gal(K/k) acts on the coefficients of φ, write φ^σ ∈ K(t) for the resulting rational function. If τ_σ : ℙ¹_K → ℙ¹_K is the standard Galois action of σ on ℙ¹_K, then we have φ^σ = τ_σ ∘ φ ∘ τ_σ⁻¹.

If σ is the image of f ∈ 𝒩_φ, then
φ^σ = τ_σ ∘ φ ∘ τ_σ⁻¹ = (τ_σ ∘ f⁻¹) ∘ φ ∘ (τ_σ ∘ f⁻¹)⁻¹.

Since τ_σ ∘ f⁻¹ ∈ PGL₂(K) is K-linear, this implies that σ ∈ Gal(K/k_ξ).

On the other hand, if σ ∈ Gal(K/k_ξ), then there exists f ∈ PGL₂(K) such that φ^σ = f ∘ φ ∘ f⁻¹. Since φ^σ = τ_σ ∘ φ ∘ τ_σ⁻¹, this implies that τ_σ ∘ f⁻¹ is an element of 𝒩_φ mapping to σ. □

Recall that, since d ≥ 2, the stabilizer 𝒜_φ is finite [Sil95, Proposition 4.1], hence 𝒩_φ is pro-finite. There are natural actions of 𝒩_φ on ℙ¹_K and Spec K; denote by 𝒫_ξ^D, 𝒢_ξ^D the quotient stacks [ℙ¹_K/𝒩_φ], [Spec K/𝒩_φ].

If the reader finds quotients by pro-finite groups uncomfortable, notice that we can find a large finite Galois extension k′/k where everything is defined: we can then proceed to repeat the above with k′ instead of K, so that all the groups involved are finite. In particular, this shows that 𝒫_ξ^D and 𝒢_ξ^D are Deligne–Mumford stacks over k_ξ. Furthermore, 𝒢_ξ^D is gerbe over k_ξ, since k_ξ is perfect and 𝒩_φ maps surjectively on Gal(K/k_ξ) by Lemma 4.

We use the superscript •^D to distinguish the Deligne–Mumford stacks 𝒫_ξ^D and 𝒢_ξ^D from their non-Deligne–Mumford siblings 𝒫_ξ and 𝒢_ξ that will appear in §4, and which will be used to study the problem over non-perfect fields.

Choose coordinates on \( \mathbf{P}^1_K \) so that \( 0, \infty \) are the two fixed points for the action of \( \mathcal{A}_\xi \). Let \( n \) be the order of \( \mathcal{A}_\phi \), then \( \mathcal{A}_\xi \) acts on \( \mathbf{P}^1_K \) by \( x \mapsto \zeta_n x \), where \( \zeta_n \) is a primitive \( n \)-th root of 1. The fact that \( \mathcal{A}_\phi \) stabilizes \( \phi \) implies that \( \phi \) has the form

\[
\phi(x) = \lambda x^{a} \prod_{i=0}^{n-1} \frac{q_1(\zeta_n^i x)}{q_2(\zeta_n^i x)}
\]

for some \( \lambda \in K \), \( a \in \mathbb{Z} \) and \( q_1, q_2 \in K[x] \).

Notice that, since \( x \mapsto \zeta_n x \) stabilizes \( \phi \), we get the equation \( \phi(\zeta_n x) = \zeta_n \phi(x) \). Applying this to the formula above, we get

\[
\zeta_n = \zeta_n^a,
\]

i.e. \( a \equiv 1 \pmod{n} \).

Let us now prove that

\[
2a + n \deg q_1 - n \deg q_2 = 0.
\]

Equivalently, we want to prove that the orders of vanishing of \( \phi \) on \( 0 \) and \( \infty \) are equal.

Notice that the subset \( \{0, \infty\} \subset \mathbf{P}^1_K \) is distinguished in the sense of [Bre23, §7]: if \( h \in \mathcal{N}_\phi \) and \( g \in \mathcal{A}_\phi \), then \( g(h(0)) = h(h^{-1} g h(0)) = h(0) \) and hence \( h(0) \) is a fixed point for \( \mathcal{A}_\phi \). If the orders of vanishing of \( \phi \) on \( 0 \) and \( \infty \) are different, then \( \{0\} \) is a distinguished subset as well, hence by [Bre23, Lemma 17] the image of \( 0 \) in \( \mathbf{P}^1_K/\mathcal{A}_\xi \) descends to a \( k_\xi \)-rational point of \( \mathbf{P}^D_\xi \), which is absurd.

Recall that we are assuming that \( \mathcal{A}_\phi \) is non-trivial, or equivalently \( n \neq 1 \). Since \( a \equiv 1 \pmod{n} \) and \( 2a + n\deg q_1 - n\deg q_2 = 0 \), the only possibility is \( n = 2 \). Hence, we get the equality \( a = \deg q_2 - \deg q_1 \) and the formula

\[
\phi(x) = \lambda x^{\deg q_2 - \deg q_1} \frac{q_1(x)q_1(-x)}{q_2(x)q_2(-x)}.
\]

This implies that \( \phi \) has degree equal to \( 2 \max(\deg q_1, \deg q_2) \). By Silverman's theorem, this implies that \( \phi \) descends to \( \mathbf{P}^1_{k_\xi} \), giving the desired contradiction.

Remark 13. The gerbe 𝔾^𝔻_ξ constructed in §1 is the rigidification of 𝔾_ξ [AGV08, Appendix C] by the connected component of the inertia stack. It follows that the induced morphism 𝔾_ξ → 𝔾^𝔻_ξ is a relative gerbe with connected relative inertia. If k is perfect, it can be proved that such a morphism induces a bijection on isomorphism classes of rational points: this is the reason why the simpler formalism used in the previous sections works when k is perfect.

Remark 14. It is natural to ask if we can drop the assumption that ξ contains a morphism defined over K. We only use this assumption to define the field of moduli and to prove that 𝔾_ξ is an algebraic stack. Even if ξ does not contain a morphism defined over K, if we know that 𝔾_ξ is an algebraic stack then the field of moduli can be defined as the coarse moduli space of 𝔾_ξ [BV24, Proposition 3.10] and Theorem 12 holds in this generality.
