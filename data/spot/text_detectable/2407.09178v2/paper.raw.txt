Let \(X\) be a compact hyperkähler manifold with a Lagrangian fibration \(\pi: X \to B\). A Shafarevich–Tate twist of \(X\) is a holomorphic symplectic manifold with a Lagrangian fibration \(\pi^\varphi: X^\varphi \to B\) which is isomorphic to \(\pi\) locally over the base. In particular, \(\pi^\varphi\) has the same fibers as \(\pi\). A twist \(X^\varphi\) corresponds to an element \(\varphi\) in the Shafarevich–Tate group Ш of \(X\). We show that \(X^\varphi\) is Kähler when a multiple of \(\varphi\) lies in the connected component of unity of Ш and give a necessary condition for \(X^\varphi\) to be bimeromorphic to a Kähler manifold.

**Definition 1.1.1.** An irreducible holomorphic symplectic manifold \(X\) is a compact complex simply connected manifold admitting a closed holomorphic symplectic form \(\sigma\) such that \(H^0(\Omega^2_X) = \mathbb{C} \cdot \sigma\). If \(X\) is Kähler, then we will call \(X\) irreducible hyperkähler\textsuperscript{1}.

Definition 1.1.2. A Lagrangian fibration on an irreducible holomorphic symplectic manifold \( X \) is a morphism \( \pi: X \to B \) with connected fibers to a normal variety \( B \) such that the restriction of \( \sigma \) to a smooth fiber is zero.

If \( X \) is hyperkähler and the base \( B \) is smooth, then \( B \) is necessarily isomorphic to \( \mathbb{P}^n \) [Hwa08]. No examples of Lagrangian fibrations on irreducible holomorphic symplectic manifolds over a base other than \( \mathbb{P}^n \) have been discovered and conjecturally the base should always be \( \mathbb{P}^n \).

Definition 1.1.3. Define the sheaf \( T_{X/B} \) of vertical vector fields on \( X \) as the kernel of the map \( T_X \to \pi^*T_B/\mathrm{Tors}(\pi^*T_B) \), where \( T_B := (\Omega_B)^\vee \) and \( \mathrm{Tors}(\pi^*T_B) \) is the torsion subsheaf of \( \pi^*T_B \).$^2$

The flow of a vertical vector field \( v \) induces a vertical automorphism \( \exp(v) \) of \( X \).

Definition 1.1.4. Consider the sheaf \( \mathrm{Aut}^0_{X/B} \) on \( B \) consisting of all vertical automorphisms that are of the form \( \exp(v) \) for some vertical vector field \( v \) locally over \( B \). The Shafarevich–Tate group of the fibration \( \pi: X \to B \) is defined to be the group \( \Sha = H^1(B, \mathrm{Aut}^0_{X/B}) \).$^3$

The group \( \Sha \) has a beautiful geometric interpretation. Cover \( B \) by open disks so that \( B = \bigcup U_i \). For each subset \( I \) of indices, we denote \( \bigcap_{i \in I} U_i \) by \( U_I \) and \( \pi^{-1}(U_I) \) by \( X_I \). Every class \( \varphi \in \Sha \) can be represented by a Čech 1-cocycle with coefficients in \( \mathrm{Aut}^0_{X/B} \). In other words, we have a vertical automorphism \( \varphi_{ij} \) of \( X_{ij} \) for each pair of indices \( i, j \), and
\[
\varphi_{ik} = \varphi_{jk} \circ \varphi_{ij}. \tag{1}
\]

For each \( i, j \) glue \( X_i \) to \( X_j \) by the automorphism \( \varphi_{ij} \) to get a new variety \( X^\varphi \). By the cocycle condition (1) the variety \( X^\varphi \) is a smooth Hausdorff complex manifold admitting a fibration
\[
\pi^\varphi : X^\varphi \to B.
\]

Definition 1.1.5. The manifold \( X^\varphi \) constructed above is called the Shafarevich–Tate twist of \( X \) with respect to the class \( \varphi \in \Sha \).

Note that the sheaves \( \mathrm{Aut}^0_{X/B} \) and \( \mathrm{Aut}^0_{X^\varphi/B} \) are isomorphic. Hence the Shafarevich–Tate group of \( \pi: X \to B \) is the same as the Shafarevich–Tate group of \( \pi^\varphi: X^\varphi \to B \). The Shafarevich–Tate twist of \( X^\varphi \) with respect to \( \psi \in \Sha \) is isomorphic to \( X^{\varphi+\psi} \).

The Shafarevich–Tate group \( \Sha = H^1(B, \mathrm{Aut}^0_{X/B}) \) has a structure of a topological group, possibly non-Hausdorff [AR21, Subsection 3.1]. Denote its connected component of unity by \( \Sha^0 \). By Theorem 2.2.7 the group \( \Sha^0 \) is a quotient of \( \mathbb{C} \) by a finitely generated subgroup. By [AR21, Subsection 6.3] the discrete part \( \Sha/\Sha^0 \) of \( \Sha \) satisfies:
\[
(\Sha/\Sha^0) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \cong H^2(R^1\pi_* \mathbb{Q}).
\]
For a class \( \varphi \in \Sha \), we will denote by \( \bar{\varphi} \) its image in \( \Sha/\Sha^0 \otimes \mathbb{Q} \). We will denote by \( \Sha' \) the set of classes \( \varphi \in \Sha \) such that \( \bar{\varphi} = 0 \).

Definition 1.1.6. A Shafarevich–Tate deformation is a Shafarevich–Tate twist \( X^\varphi \) of \( X \) with respect to an element \( \varphi \in \Sha^0 \).

Theorem A (3.0.6, Theorem 3.0.7). Let \( \pi: X \to B \) be a Lagrangian fibration on an irreducible hyper-kähler manifold \( X \). Pick a class \( \varphi \in \Sha' \), i.e., a class \( \varphi \) such that \( r\varphi \) lies in \( \Sha^0 \) for some positive integer \( r \). Then the following holds.

1. The twist \( X^\varphi \) is Kähler.
2. A twist \( X^\varphi \) with respect to \( \varphi \in \Sha' \) is projective if and only if \( \varphi \) is torsion.

We proved a version of this theorem in [AR21, Theorem B] for a general hyperkähler manifold, assuming \( \pi \) has no multiple fibers in codimension one [AR21, Definition 2.3] and \( \varphi \in \Sha^0 \). The new proof does not require any of these assumptions.

Remark 1.2.1. A weaker version of Theorem A recently appeared in [SV24]. However, our arguments are different, and we prove a more general statement.

$^2$ When \( B \) is smooth, the sheaf \( \pi^*T_B \) is clearly locally free, hence torsion free. We do not know whether \( \pi^*T_B \) is torsion free in general.

$^3$ \( \Sha \) is a letter of the Russian alphabet pronounced as "Sha". It is the first letter in the last name Шафаревич (Shafarevich).

1.2.2. A Shafarevich–Tate twist X^φ of a holomorphic symplectic manifold is holomorphic symplectic, and the fibration π^φ is a Lagrangian fibration [AR21, Corollary 3.7]. We can show more.

**Theorem B** (5.2.9). Let π: X → B be a Lagrangian fibration on an irreducible hyperkähler manifold. Then for any φ ∈ Ш we have H^0(X^φ, Ω^2_{X^φ}) = ℂ · σ, where σ is a holomorphic symplectic form on X^φ. Moreover, H^1(X^φ, ℚ) = 0.

1.2.3. In the next theorem we compute the second Betti number of Shafarevich–Tate twists. Note that the differential d₂ on the second page of the Leray spectral sequence of ℚ_X for the map π maps H^0(B, R^2π_*ℚ) to H^2(B, R^1π_*ℚ) ≅ (Ш/Ш⁰) ⊗ ℚ.

**Theorem C** (5.3.11). Let π: X → B be a Lagrangian fibration on an irreducible hyperkähler manifold X and φ ∈ Ш. Then exactly one of the following two cases occurs.

1. If the image φ̄ of φ in H^2(R^1π_*ℚ) lies in the image of d₂, then b_2(X^φ) = b_2(X). Moreover, there is a cohomology class h ∈ H^2(X^φ) which restricts to an ample class on a smooth fiber.

2. If φ̄ is not in the image of d₂, then b_2(X^φ) = b_2(X) − 1. In this case all cohomology classes h ∈ H^2(X^φ) restrict trivially to a smooth fiber.

**Definition 1.2.4.** A complex manifold is said to be of Fujiki class 𝒞 if it is bimeromorphic to a Kähler manifold.

We will derive the following criterion for non-Kählerness of Shafarevich–Tate twists as an easy corollary of Theorem C.

**Theorem D** (5.3.12). Let π: X → B be a Lagrangian fibration on a hyperkähler manifold X. Pick φ ∈ Ш such that φ̄ is not in the image of d₂. Then X^φ is not of Fujiki class 𝒞, in particular, not Kähler.

1.2.5. **Outline of the paper.** We start by recalling basic facts about Lagrangian fibrations and their Shafarevich–Tate twists in Section 2. Many results in Section 2 were contained in our previous work [AR21] but were stated assuming that the base B of a Lagrangian fibration π is smooth and π has no multiple fibers in codimension one. We show that these assumptions are not necessary. In Section 3 we will prove the second part of Theorem A, which is easier than the first part. The first part of Theorem A will be proven in Section 4. In Section 5 we study cohomological properties of Shafarevich–Tate twists. We will see that Shafarevich–Tate twists have trivial first cohomology in Subsection 5.1 and prove that H^0(Ω^2_{X^φ}) is one-dimensional in Subsection 5.2. These two statements immediately imply Theorem B. Finally, in Subsection 5.3 we prove Theorem C and then show how to derive Theorem D from Theorem C.

## 2 Preliminaries

### 2.1 Lagrangian fibrations

2.1.1. **Beauville–Bogomolov–Fujiki form.** One of the key cohomological features of hyperkähler manifold is the existence of a quadratic form on their second cohomology called *Beauville–Bogomolov–Fujiki form* (BBF form).

Theorem 2.1.2 ([GJH03, Part III, Corollary 23.11 & Proposition 23.14]). Let X be an irreducible hyperkähler manifold of dimension 2n. Then there exists an integral symmetric non-degenerate form q on H²(X) such that ∀α ∈ H²(X, ℤ),

c_X q(α)^n = ∫_X α^{2n}.

The constant c_X is positive and depends only on the deformation type of X.

Remark 2.1.3. The integral form q from Theorem 2.1.2 is uniquely defined if we require it to be non-divisible.

Definition 2.1.4. The form q from Theorem 2.1.2 is called the Beauville–Bogomolov–Fujiki form or BBF form.

2.1.5. Fibers of Lagrangian fibrations are abelian varieties. Consider a Lagrangian fibration π: X → B (Definition 1.1.2) on an irreducible holomorphic symplectic manifold X. A general fiber of π is a complex torus and even an abelian variety [Cam06]. The projectivity of smooth fibers follows easily from the theorem below.

Theorem 2.1.6 ([Voi92, Mat99]). Let π: X → B be a Lagrangian fibration on a hyperkähler manifold and F its smooth fiber. Then the restriction map

H²(X, ℚ) → H²(F, ℚ)

has rank one.

Thanks to Theorem 2.1.6, for any Kähler class h ∈ H²(X, ℝ) some real multiple c · h of h restricts to an integral class on F. The class c · h|_F is Kähler and integral, hence ample. It follows that F is indeed an abelian variety.

2.1.7. Discriminant. The image in B of singular fibers of π is called the discriminant of the Lagrangian fibration and will be denoted by Δ. It is known to be a divisor [HO09, Proposition 3.1]. We define B° to be the complement of Δ and X° := π⁻¹(B°).

2.1.8. Vertical vector fields. The holomorphic symplectic form σ enables us to construct a lot of vertical vector fields on X. First, it induces an isomorphism Ω_X →^{ι_σ} T_X. Let X' denote the subset π⁻¹(B^reg) ⊂ X. Consider the composition of maps

π^*Ω_{B^reg} → Ω_{X'} →^{ι_σ} T_{X'} → π^*T_{B^reg}.

It is easy to see that it vanishes on X°. Indeed, for every form α on an open subset of the base, the vector field dual to π^*α is tangent to smooth fibers of π. Since π^*T_{B^reg} is locally free, the map π^*Ω_{B^reg} → π^*T_{B^reg} vanishes on X'. Therefore, the map ι_σ sends π^*Ω_{B^reg} into T_{X'/B^reg} (Definition 1.1.3). By taking pushforwards to B^reg we obtain a map

π_*π^*Ω_{B^reg} → π_*T_{X'/B^reg}.

By [Mat99] π_*𝒪_X ≅ 𝒪_B. The projection formula implies that π_*π^*Ω_{B^reg} ≅ Ω_{B^reg}, and we get a map:

Ω_{B^reg} → π_*T_{X'/B^reg}. (2)

The sheaf T_{X/B} is the kernel of the map T_X → π^*T_B / Tors(π^*T_B) (Definition 1.1.3). The kernel of a map of a reflexive sheaf to a torsion-free sheaf is a reflexive sheaf, hence T_{X/B} is reflexive. The pushforward of a reflexive sheaf along an equidimensional morphism is reflexive [Har80, Corollary 1.7], hence π_*T_{X/B} is reflexive as well. Therefore, the map (2) extends to a map

ι_σ : Ω_B^{[1]} → π_*T_{X/B}. (3)

Here Ω_B^{[1]} denotes the sheaf of reflexive differentials on B, i.e., the double dual of Ω_B. Equivalently, Ω_B^{[1]} := j_*Ω_{B^reg}, where j: B^reg ↪ B is the embedding of the smooth locus of B into B. Similarly, we define Ω_B^{[i]} as j_*Ω_{B^reg}^i.

2.1.9. The map (3) turns out to be an isomorphism. We showed this fact in [AR21, Theorem 2.6] assuming that \(B\) is smooth and the fibration \(\pi\) has no multiple fibers in codimension one. These assumptions are not necessary, as we will see very soon. The proof relies on the following elementary lemma.

Lemma 2.1.10. Let \(\pi: Y \rightarrow S\) be a flat morphism of finite type of possibly non-compact complex manifolds. As before, denote by \(S^\circ\) the image of smooth fibers of \(\pi\) and by \(Y^\circ\) the preimage of \(S^\circ\) in \(Y\). Let \(\Delta := S \setminus S^\circ\) be the discriminant locus of \(\pi\). Suppose that \(\alpha\) is a holomorphic k-form on \(Y\) such that the restriction of \(\alpha\) to \(Y^\circ\) satisfies
\[
\alpha|_{Y^\circ} = \pi^* \beta^\circ 
\]
for some holomorphic k-form \(\beta^\circ\) on \(S^\circ\). Then the form \(\beta^\circ\) extends to a holomorphic k-form \(\beta\) on \(S\) and \(\alpha = \pi^* \beta\).

Proof. Suppose that \(\alpha|_{\pi^{-1}(S')}\) = \(\pi^* \beta'\) for some form \(\beta'\) on an open subset \(S' \subset S\) with complement of codimension at least two. Then we are done. Indeed, by Hartogs theorem \(\beta'\) extends to a holomorphic form \(\beta\) on \(S\). The forms \(\pi^* \beta\) and \(\alpha\) coincide on an open subset, hence they coincide on \(Y\). Therefore, it is enough to prove the statement for some \(S' \subset S\) as above.

If \(\mathrm{codim}\; \Delta \geq 2\), then we are done, so let us assume that \(\mathrm{codim}\; \Delta = 1\). Pick a general point \(b \in \Delta\). Let \(U\) be a neighborhood of \(b\). It is enough to show that \(\beta^\circ\) extends to a holomorphic form on \(U\). The fibration \(\pi\) might not admit a local section in a neighborhood \(U\) of \(b\); yet, for some finite cover \(f: V \rightarrow U\) ramified in \(\Delta \cap U\), the base change morphism \(\pi_V: X_V \rightarrow V\) of \(\pi\) to \(V\) admits a section. Call this section \(s: V \rightarrow X_V\), and denote the map \(X_V \rightarrow X\) by \(F\). We obtain the following diagram

\[
\begin{array}{ccc}
X_V & \xrightarrow{F} & X_U \rightarrow X \\
\downarrow \pi_V &         & \downarrow \pi_U~~\downarrow \pi \\
V~\xrightarrow{f}~U~\rightarrow~B \\
\uparrow s \\
X_V
\end{array}
\]

The following equality of forms on \(F^{-1}((X_U)^\circ)\) holds:
\[
F^* \alpha|_{F^{-1}((X_U)^\circ)} = \pi_V^* f^* \beta^\circ.
\]
It follows that the form \(s^* F^* \alpha\) coincides with \(f^* \beta^\circ\) on \(V^\circ\). Therefore \(f^* \beta^\circ\) can be extended to a form \(\beta_V := s^* F^* \alpha\) on \(V\). As we will see in a moment, this implies that \(\beta^\circ\) extends to a holomorphic form on \(U\). Indeed, choose coordinates \((t, z_1, \ldots, z_{n-1})\) on \(U\) and \((s, z_1, \ldots, z_{n-1})\) on \(V\) such that \(\Delta \cap U = \{t = 0\}\) and the map \(f\) sends \((s, z_1, \ldots, z_{n-1})\) to \((s^k, z_1, \ldots, z_{n-1})\). Write
\[
\beta^\circ = hdt + \sum_{i=1}^{k-1} h_i dz_i
\]
for some functions \(h\) and \(h_i\) on \(U^\circ\). Then
\[
f^* \beta^\circ = k h(s^k, z) s^{k-1} ds + \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} h_i dz_i.
\]
The form \(f^* \beta^\circ\) extends to a holomorphic form on \(V\). Hence the functions \(h_i\)'s extend to holomorphic functions on \(V\). They are bounded on \(V\), hence bounded on \(U\). Therefore, \(h_i\)'s extend to holomorphic functions on \(U\). The function \(h(s^k, z) s^{k-1}\) is also bounded, hence so is
\[
h(t, z)t = h(s^k, z)s^k.
\]
Therefore, \(h\) has at worst a simple pole at \(\Delta\). But the form
\[
f^* \frac{dt}{t} = k \frac{ds}{s},
\]
is not holomorphic. Hence \(h\) is actually holomorphic on \(U\). It follows that \(\beta^\circ\) extends to a holomorphic form on \(U\).

Theorem 2.1.11. The map \(\iota_\sigma: \Omega_B^{[1]} \to \pi_* T_{X/B}\) is an isomorphism.

Proof. This map is definitely an isomorphism over \(B^\circ\) and is injective (2.1.8). It is enough to show that it is surjective. Let \(v\) be a vertical vector field over an open subset \(U \subset B\). Then the form \(\iota_v \sigma\) equals \(\pi^*\beta^\circ\) for some holomorphic 1-form \(\beta^\circ\) on \(B^\circ \cap U\). By Lemma 2.1.10, the form \(\beta^\circ\) extends to a holomorphic form \(\beta\) on \(U^{reg}\) and \(\iota_v \sigma|_{\pi^{-1}(U^{reg})}\) coincides with \(\pi^*\beta\). Hence the map \(\iota_\sigma\) sends the form \(\beta\), considered as a section of \(\Omega_B^{[1]}\) over \(U\), to \(v\).

2.1.12. Higher pushforwards of \(\mathcal{O}_X\) When the base \(B\) of a Lagrangian fibration is smooth, the higher pushforward sheaves \(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X\) are locally free [Mat05]. Without the smoothness assumption one can show that the sheaves \(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X\) are reflexive for all \(i \geq 0\) [Ou19, Proposition 3.6]. Let \(\omega\) a Kähler form on \(X\). Consider the composition of maps

\[
\Omega_B^{[1]} \xrightarrow{\iota_\sigma} \pi_* T_{X/B} \xrightarrow{f_\omega} R^1\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X.
\]

Here \(f_\omega\) sends a vertical vector field \(v\) to the class \([\iota_v\omega]_{\bar\partial}\) of the \(\bar\partial\)-closed \((0,1)\)-form \(\iota_v\omega\) under the \(\bar\partial\)-differential.

Theorem 2.1.13 ([Ou19], [Mat05]). Let \(\pi: X \to B\) be a Lagrangian fibration on a projective manifold. Then the map \(\Omega_B^{[1]} \to R^1\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X\) and the induced maps \(\Omega_B^{[i]} \to R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X\) are isomorphisms.

Corollary 2.1.14. Let \(\pi: X \to B\) be a Lagrangian fibration on an irreducible hyperkähler manifold, not necessarily projective. Then Theorem 2.1.13 holds for any Shafarevich–Tate twist \(X^\varphi\) of \(X\), in particular for \(X\) itself, i.e.,

\[
R^i\pi_*^\varphi\mathcal{O}_{X^\varphi} \simeq \Omega_B^{[i]}.
\]

Proof. By [Huy99, Theorem 3.5] any non-trivial family of deformations of an irreducible hyperkähler manifold contains a projective deformation. Therefore there exists a projective Shafarevich–Tate deformation \(\pi^\psi: X^\psi \to B\) of the Lagrangian fibration \(\pi: X \to B\). It follows from Theorem 2.1.13 that

\[
R^i\pi_*^\psi\mathcal{O}_{X^\psi} \simeq \Omega_B^{[i]}.
\]

The sheaf of groups \(\mathrm{Aut}^0_{X/B}\) acts trivially on \(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X\). Indeed, the restriction of \(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X\) to \(B^\circ\) is a vector bundle with fibers \(H^{0,i}(F)\). Automorphisms in \(\mathrm{Aut}^0_{X/B}\) act trivially on \(H^{0,i}(F)\) for any smooth fiber \(F\). Thus the action of \(\mathrm{Aut}^0_{X/B}\) on \(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X\) is trivial over \(B^\circ\). The sheaf \(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X\) is torsion-free, hence the action of \(\mathrm{Aut}^0_{X/B}\) is trivial everywhere.

We obtain that for any \(\varphi\in \Sha\)
\[
R^i\pi_*^\varphi\mathcal{O}_{X^\varphi} \simeq R^i\pi_*^\psi\mathcal{O}_{X^\psi} \simeq \Omega_B^{[i]}.
\]

Remark 2.1.15. It follows from Corollary 2.1.14 that the sheaves \(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X\) are locally free on \(B^{reg}\). The base change theorem [Mum12, Chapter 5, Corollary 2&3] implies that for all points \(b \in B^{reg}\) the dimension of \(H^i(\mathcal{O}_{\pi^{-1}(b)})\) does not depend on \(b\). In particular, \(h^0(\mathcal{O}_{\pi^{-1}(b)}) = 1\) for every \(b \in B^{reg}\).

Theorem 2.1.16. Let \(B\) be the base of a Lagrangian fibration on an irreducible hyperkähler manifold \(X\). Then the cohomology groups \(H^j(B, \Omega_B^{[i]})\) are the same as for \(B = \mathbb{P}^n\).

Proof. Step 1. By Corollary 2.1.14, \(H^j(\Omega_B^{[i]}) \simeq H^j(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X)\). It follows from a result by Kollár [Kol86, p.172] that
\[
R\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X \simeq \bigoplus R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X[-i].
\]
Therefore the Leray spectral sequence for \(\mathcal{O}_X\) degenerates on \(E^2\) and
\[
h^{0,k}(X) = \sum_{i=0}^k h^{k-i}(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X).
\]

When \(k\) is odd \(h^{0,k} = 0\) and when \(k\) is even \(h^{0,k} = 1\). We see immediately that \(H^j(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X) = 0\) when \(i + j\) is odd. When \(k\) is even, there is exactly one \(i \leq k\) such that \(H^{k-i}(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X)\) is non-zero.

Step 2. We will show that \(H^i(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X)\) does not vanish. This will complete the proof. Consider the filtration \(F^i H^{0,k}(X)\) on \(H^{0,k}(X)\) induced by the Leray spectral sequence. First, consider the case \(k = 2\). The cohomology group \(H^{0,2}(X)\) is generated by \(\sigma\). The restriction of \(\sigma\) to a smooth fiber is zero, hence the image of \(\sigma\) in \(H^0(B, R^2\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X)\) vanishes. The form \(\sigma\) is non-degenerate, hence not the pullback of a \((0,2)\)-form on the base even locally. Therefore \(F^0H^{0,2}(X) = 0\) and \(F^1H^{0,2}(X) = F^2H^{0,2}(X) = H^{0,2}(X)\).

It follows that \(\sigma^i \in F^i H^{0,2i}(X)\) for all \(i\). Suppose that we know that \(\sigma^i \not\in F^{i-1}H^{0,2i}(X)\). Then \(H^i(R^i\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X) = F^iH^{0,2i}(X)/F^{i-1}H^{0,2i}(X)\) is non-zero, and we are done. If \(\sigma^i\) happens to be contained in \(F^{i-1}H^{0,2i}(X)\), then \(\sigma^n\) is contained in \(F^{n-1}H^{0,2n}(X)\). However, \(F^{n-1}H^{0,2n}(X)\) vanishes for dimension reasons. Indeed, \(H^{n+k}(R^{n-k}\pi_*\mathcal{O}_X) = 0\) for \(k > 0\). Hence \(\sigma^n = 0\), contradiction.

Remark 2.1.17. A base of a Lagrangian fibration behaves like ℙⁿ from many points of view (conjecturally because it is always ℙⁿ). We encourage an interested reader to look into the wonderful survey [HM22] for details.

## 2.2 Shafarevich–Tate group

### 2.2.1. Structure of the Shafarevich–Tate group.

Recall that the sheaf of groups Aut⁰ₓ/ᴮ is defined as the image of the exponential map π₍*₎Tₓ/ᴮ → Autₓ/ᴮ (Definition 1.1.4). Define Γ to be the kernel of this map. The short exact sequence

0 → Γ → π₍*₎Tₓ/ᴮ → Aut⁰ₓ/ᴮ → 0

induces the long exact sequence of cohomology groups:

H¹(Γ) → H¹(π₍*₎Tₓ/ᴮ) → Ш → H²(Γ).                                    (4)

We will call the image of H¹(π₍*₎Tₓ/ᴮ) in Ш the connected component of unity of Ш and will denote it by Ш⁰. The quotient Ш/Ш⁰ is the discrete part of Ш.

The sequence (4) is exact on the right. Indeed, the cohomology group H²(π₍*₎Tₓ/ᴮ) is isomorphic to H²(Ω¹ᴮ) by Theorem 2.1.11. By Theorem 2.1.16 this cohomology group vanishes. Similarly, the vector space H¹(π₍*₎Tₓ/ᴮ) is isomorphic to H¹(B, Ω¹ᴮ) and is one-dimensional.

### 2.2.2. Degenerate twistor deformations.

There is a useful differential geometric point of view on Shafarevich–Tate deformations [AR21, Subsection 2.3]. Let σ be a holomorphic symplectic form on X and α be a closed (1,1)-form on B. The form σ + tπ*α is obviously not holomorphic, but it turns out that there exists a different complex structure Iₜ on X making σ + tπ*α holomorphic symplectic [SV24, Section 2.2]. Moreover, such a complex structure is unique.

Definition 2.2.3. Denote by Xₜ the manifold X with the new complex structure Iₜ. It is called a degenerate twistor deformation of X.

It is not hard to see that the fibration π : Xₜ → B is holomorphic and Lagrangian with respect to the new complex structure.

Degenerate twistor deformations form a family

Π : 𝒳 → 𝔸¹,

and the fiber of Π over t ∈ 𝔸¹ is isomorphic to the degenerate twistor deformation Xₜ.

Definition 2.2.4 ([AR21, Definition 2.18, Definition 3.4]). The family Π : 𝒳 → 𝔸¹ is called the degenerate twistor family or the Shafarevich–Tate family.

We will see in Theorem 2.2.10 that all degenerate twistor deformations are Shafarevich–Tate deformations (Definition 1.1.6). That justifies the use of the term Shafarevich–Tate family.

3.0.3. Gluing a line bundle. The proof of Theorem 3.0.1 relies on the following idea. Pick a line bundle L on X and cover B by open disks Ui. Let Li denote the restriction of L to Xi. Lemma 3.0.4. Let L be a line bundle on an abelian variety A and t an r-torsion element of A. Then
\[ t^*L^r \cong L^r. \]

Proof. Consider the morphism φ_L : A → A^∨ sending x to x^*L ⊗ L^{-1}. The map φ_L is a homomorphism because any morphism of abelian varieties sending zero to zero is a homomorphism [Mum12, Section 4, Corollary 1]. Therefore φ_L(t) is an r-torsion line bundle, i.e.,
\[ (\varphi_L(t))^r = t^*L^r \otimes L^{-r} \cong \mathcal{O}_A. \]

Lemma 3.0.5. Let π: Y → S be a proper flat morphism between varieties such that h⁰(O_{Y_b}) = 1 for all b ∈ S outside a codimension at least two subset of S. Consider a line bundle M on Y with the following properties:
1. the restriction of M to any smooth fiber is trivial;
2. the restriction of M to any fiber Y_b lies in Pic⁰(Y_b). Here Pic⁰(Y_b) is the connected component of unity of Pic(Y_b).

Then some positive multiple M^s of M for s ∈ ℤ_{>0} is isomorphic to the pullback of a line bundle from S.

Proof. Step 1. It is enough to show this statement for some S' ⊂ S with complement of codimension at least two. Indeed, suppose that M^s|_{π^{-1}(S')} is isomorphic to π^*K' for a line bundle K' on S'. We can extend K' to a line bundle K on S. The line bundles π^*K and M^s are isomorphic outside a codimension at least two subset of Y, hence they are isomorphic.

Step 2. Denote by Δ the discriminant locus of π. If codim Δ ≥ 2, then we are done thanks to Step 1. So we may assume codim Δ = 1. Consider the group E_b ⊂ Pic(Y_b) of line bundles L on Y_b with the following property: there exists a line bundle 𝑳̃ on Y which is trivial on smooth fibers and restricts...

to L on \( Y_b \). By Raynaud's theorem [Ray70, Introduction], \( E_b \) has dimension \( h^0(\mathcal{O}_{Y_b}) - 1 \) for a general point \( b \in \Delta \). The assumption that \( h^0(\mathcal{O}_{Y_b}) = 1 \) for a general point \( b \in \Delta \) implies that \( E_b \) is discrete for any fiber of \( \pi \) over a general point \( b \in \Delta \). The line bundle \( M_b := M|_{Y_b} \) is in \( E_b \) by the first property.

By the second assumption, \( M_b \in \mathrm{Pic}^0(Y_b) \). Consider the group \( \langle M_b \rangle \) generated by \( M_b \) inside \( \mathrm{Pic}^0(Y_b) \). It is contained inside \( E_b \), hence is discrete. Since the group space \( \mathrm{Pic}^0(Y_b) \) is of finite type [FGI+05, Proposition 9.5.3], the group \( \langle M_b \rangle \) is of finite type as well. Hence \( \langle M_b \rangle \) is finite, in other words, \( M_b \) is torsion. Therefore, some power \( M^s \) of \( M \) restricts trivially to all fibers over \( S' \subset S \) with complement of codimension at least two. Define a line bundle \( K' := \pi_* M^s|_{\pi^{-1}(S')} \). The natural map \( \pi^* K' \to M^s|_{\pi^{-1}(S')} \) is an isomorphism.

Proof of Theorem A (2). Step 1. Pick a line bundle \( L \) on \( X \). As before, choose a Čech cocycle \( (\varphi_{ij}) \) with \( r \varphi_{ij} = 0 \) representing an \( r \)-torsion class \( \varphi \in \Sha \). We will construct an isomorphism:

for some \( s \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0} \). The line bundle \( \varphi_{ij}^* L^r \otimes L^{-r} \) on \( X_{ij} \) restricts trivially to smooth fibers by Lemma 3.0.4. Moreover, it satisfies the second condition of Lemma 3.0.5 because \( \varphi_{ij} \in \mathrm{Aut}^0_{X/B} \). For every \( b \in B^{reg} \) the fibers \( \pi^{-1}(b) \) satisfy \( h^0(\mathcal{O}_{\pi^{-1}(b)}) = 1 \), see 2.1.12. Hence the restriction of some multiple of \( \varphi_{ij}^* L^r \otimes L^{-r} \) to \( \pi^{-1}(B^{reg} \cap U_{ij}) \) is the pullback of a line bundle on \( B^{reg} \cap U_{ij} \) (Lemma 3.0.5). Since the codimension of the complement of \( B^{reg} \) in \( B \) is at least two, some multiple of \( \varphi_{ij}^* L^r \otimes L^{-r} \) is actually the pullback of a line bundle on \( U_{ij} \). When the subsets \( U_i \)'s are sufficiently small, all line bundles on \( U_{ij} \)'s are trivial. Therefore the sheaves \( \varphi_{ij}^* L^s|_{\pi^{-1}(U_{ij})} \) and \( L^s|_{\pi^{-1}(U_{ij})} \) are isomorphic.


Step 2. The isomorphisms \( f_{ij} \) might not a priori satisfy the cocycle condition. In other words, the following map
\[
f_{ij}^{-1} \circ \varphi_{ij}^* f_{jk}^{-1} \circ f_{ik}
\]
is some automorphism of \( L|_{U_{ijk}} \), which might not be trivial. Denote it by \( \lambda_{ijk} \). The automorphism \( \lambda_{ijk} \) is a multiplication by a non-zero holomorphic function on \( X_{ijk} \), which must be the pullback of a function on the base. Therefore the automorphisms \( \lambda_{ijk} \) define a Čech 2-cocycle on \( B \) with coefficients in \( \mathcal{O}_B^\times \).

Consider the following chunk of the long exact sequence of cohomology of the exponential exact sequence on \( B \):
\[
H^2(B, \mathcal{O}_B) \to H^2(B, \mathcal{O}_B^\times) \to H^3(B, \mathbb{Z}) \to H^3(B, \mathcal{O}_B).
\]
By Theorem 2.1.16, the cohomology groups \( H^2(B,\mathcal{O}_B) \) and \( H^3(B, \mathcal{O}_B) \) vanish. Hence \( H^2(B, \mathcal{O}_B^\times) \cong H^3(B, \mathbb{Z}) \). The cohomology groups \( H^i(B, \mathbb{Q}) \) are the same as for \( \mathbb{P}^n \) [SY22, Theorem 0.2], in particular \( H^3(B, \mathbb{Z}) \) is torsion. Hence some power, say \( s' \), of the cocycle \( (\lambda_{ijk}) \) vanishes. Replace the line bundle \( L^s \) with \( L^{ss'} \) and the isomorphisms \( f_{ij} \) with \( f_{ij}^{\otimes s'} \). Then \( \lambda_{ijk} \) gets replaced with \( \lambda_{ijk}^{s'} \), which is a coboundary. Write \( \lambda_{ijk}^{s'} = \mu_{ij}\mu_{jk}\mu_{ki} \) for some \( \mu_{ij} \in \mathcal{O}_B^{\times}(U_{ij}) \). Then the isomorphisms \( (\mu_{ij}^{-1} \cdot f_{ij}) \) satisfy the cocycle condition. It follows that we can glue the line bundles \( L^s|_{\pi^{-1}(U_{ij})} \) into a global line bundle \( L^\varphi \) on \( X^\varphi \).

The line bundle \( L^\varphi \) depends only on the choice of \( \mu_{ij} \in \mathcal{O}_B^{\times}(U_{ij}) \). Different choices of \( \mu_{ij} \) differ by a 1-cocycle with coefficients in \( \mathcal{O}_B^{\times}(U_{ij}) \). Therefore, \( L^\varphi \) is well-defined up to the pullback of a line bundle on \( B \). We construct a map
\[
NS_{\mathbb{Q}}(X)/\eta \to NS_{\mathbb{Q}}(X^\varphi)/\eta
\]
by sending the class of \( L \) in \( NS_{\mathbb{Q}}(X)/\eta \) to the class \( [L^\varphi]/(ss') \in NS_{\mathbb{Q}}(X^\varphi)/\eta \).


Step 3. The restriction of \( L^\varphi \) to \( X^\varphi_i \) coincides with a power of \( L_i \). Therefore the class of \( L^\varphi \) in \( NS_{\mathbb{Q}}(X) \) has trivial restriction to smooth fibers if and only if this is true for \( L \), and \( L^\varphi \) is relatively ample if and only if so is \( L \).

Proposition 4.0.1. Let π: X → B be a Lagrangian fibration on an irreducible holomorphic symplectic manifold. Consider the restriction X → 𝔻 of its Shafarevich–Tate family to a disk 𝔻 ⊂ ℂ^1. It follows from Corollary 3.0.8 that every Shafarevich–Tate twist X^φ with respect to φ ∈ Ш' is a limit of hyperkähler manifolds in the sense of the following definition.

Definition 4.1.1. Let X be a compact complex manifold. Consider a family of deformations 𝒳 → T of X, and let 0 ∈ T be the point corresponding to X. The manifold X is said to be a limit of Kähler manifolds if for some family of deformations 𝒳 → T there is a sequence of points t_n ∈ T converging to 0 such that the deformation X_{t_n} is a Kähler manifold.

A limit of Kähler manifolds does not have to be Kähler, however the following is expected to be true.

Conjecture 1. [Pop11] A limit of Kähler manifolds is of Fujiki class C, i.e., is bimeromorphic to a Kähler manifold.

Arvid Perego in [Per17] showed that this conjecture holds for holomorphic symplectic manifolds with some additional assumptions.

Theorem 4.1.2 ([Per17, Theorem 1.14]). Let (X, σ) be a compact holomorphic symplectic b₂-manifold, i.e.,
\[ h^{2,0}(X) + h^{1,1}(X) + h^{0,2}(X) = b_2(X). \]
with a holomorphic symplectic form σ, which is a limit of irreducible hyperkähler manifolds. Then X is bimeromorphic to an irreducible hyperkähler manifold, in particular, it is of Fujiki class C.

We will use some of Perego’s ideas in the proof of Theorem A(1).

Before we get started with the proof of Proposition 4.0.1, we will sketch its main steps below.

Step 1. Period map and Torelli theorems. (Subsection 4.3). Using Local and Global Torelli Theorems (Theorem 4.3.4), we construct a family
\[\mathcal{Y} \to \mathbb{D}\]
such that \(\mathcal{Y}_t\) is hyperkähler for all t ∈ 𝔻 and \(X_t\) is bimeromorphic to \(\mathcal{Y}_t\) for all t ∈ U ⊂ 𝔻 (Lemma 4.3.6).

Step 2. Lagrangian fibration on  \(\mathcal{Y}_t\). (Subsection 4.4). Let t be a very general point in U. We will show in Corollary 4.4.3 that \(\mathcal{Y}_t\) admits a Lagrangian fibration \(p_t: \mathcal{Y}_t \to B'\), and every bimeromorphism \(f_t: X_t \dashrightarrow \mathcal{Y}_t\) commutes with the Lagrangian fibrations on \(X_t\) and \(\mathcal{Y}_t\). This step relies on a result by Greb-Lehn-Rollenske [GLR13]. Namely, they proved that a non-projective hyperkähler manifold containing a Lagrangian torus admits a Lagrangian fibration.

Step 3. \(\mathcal{Y} \to \mathbb{D}\) is almost a Shafarevich–Tate family. (Subsection 4.5). We will see in Proposition 4.5.1 that the family \(\mathcal{Y} \to \mathbb{D}\) is a Shafarevich–Tate family after restriction to some open dense subset \(V \subset U\). Moreover, it will turn out that the base \(B'\) of the Lagrangian fibration \(p_t: \mathcal{Y}_t \to B'\) for t ∈ V is isomorphic to B (Proposition 4.5.2).

Step 4. \(\mathcal{Y}_0\) is bimeromorphic to a degenerate twistor deformation of \(\mathcal{Y}_t\). (Subsection 4.6). Let \(\mathcal{Y}' \to \mathbb{D}\) be the Shafarevich–Tate family of a Lagrangian fibration \(p_\tau : \mathcal{Y}_\tau \to B\) for some τ ∈ V. By the previous step, \(\mathcal{Y}_t \simeq \mathcal{Y}'_t\) for all t ∈ V. Essentially the same argument as the one used by Perego in his proof of [Per17, Lemma 2.6] will show that \(\mathcal{Y} := \mathcal{Y}_0\) is bimeromorphic to \(\mathcal{Y}' := \mathcal{Y}'_0\) (Lemma 4.6.1). Therefore, \(\mathcal{Y}'\) is of Fujiki class C.

Step 5. Shafarevich–Tate deformations of bimeromorphic Lagrangian fibrations are bimeromorphic. (Subsection 4.7). We saw in Step 2 that the Lagrangian fibrations \(X_t\) and \(\mathcal{Y}_t = \mathcal{Y}'_t\) are bimeromorphic for some t ∈ V. We will see in 4.7.1 that all Shafarevich–Tate deformations of \(X_t\) and \(\mathcal{Y}'_t\) are bimeromorphic. Therefore, X is bimeromorphic to \(\mathcal{Y}'\), which is in its turn bimeromorphic to a hyperkähler manifold \(\mathcal{Y}\) (Corollary 4.7.3). Hence X is of Fujiki class C.

Step 6. Criterion for Kählerness. (Subsection 4.8). Perego discovered in [Per17, Theorem 1.15] a cohomological criterion for Kählerness of limits of hyperkähler manifolds which are of Fujiki class C. We will check that the assumptions of Perego’s criterion are satisfied for Shafarevich–Tate twists and will conclude that X is hyperkähler (Proposition 4.8.3).

4.3.1. Period map for hyperkähler manifolds. Let X be a hyperkähler manifold and Λ be a lattice isomorphic to the lattice (H²(X, ℤ), q_X), where q_X is the BBF form (Definition 2.1.4). Denote Λ_ℂ := Λ ⊗ ℂ.

Definition 4.3.2. The moduli space \(\mathcal{M}_\Lambda\) of Λ-marked hyperkähler manifolds is the moduli space of pairs (Y, g) where Y is a hyperkähler manifold and \(g: H^2(Y, \mathbb{Z}) \to \Lambda\) is an isomorphism of lattices.

Definition 4.3.3. The period map
\[
\mathrm{Per}: \mathcal{M}_\Lambda \to \mathbb{P}(\Lambda_\mathbb{C})
\]
sends the point of \(\mathcal{M}_\Lambda\) corresponding to a pair (Y, g) to the class of the line \(g(H^{2,0}(Y)) \subset \Lambda_\mathbb{C}\). The image of a pair (Y, g) under the period map is called its period.

**Theorem 4.3.4.**  
1. The image of the period map is contained in the subset Ω_Λ consisting of [σ] ∈ ℙ(Λ_ℂ) such that  
q(σ) = 0  and  q(σ, \overline{σ}) > 0.

2. (Local Torelli Theorem [Bea83]) The period map is a local biholomorphism onto Ω_ℂ.

3. (Global Torelli Theorem [Huy99, Theorem 8.1], [Huy11, Corollary 6.1]). Let M^0_Λ be a connected component of M_Λ. Then the period map  
Per : M^0_Λ → Ω_Λ  
is surjective. Moreover, two points (X, g) and (X′, g′) of M^0_Λ have the same periods if and only if there exists a bimeromorphism f : X → X′ such that the pullback map f^∗ : H^2(X′) → H^2(X) coincides with g^{-1} ∘ g.

Before stating it, let us recall that the Mori cone of a compact complex manifold X is the cone NE(X) ⊂ H₂(X, ℝ) generated by classes of curves on X. For any morphism X → Y we define the relative Mori cone NE(X/Y) ⊂ H₂(X, ℝ) as the cone generated by classes of curves contained in fibers of X → Y.

Corollary 4.8.2. Let X be a compact holomorphic symplectic manifold of Fujiki class C which is a limit of hyperkähler manifolds. Assume that there is a class β ∈ H¹,¹(X) satisfying the following two properties:

1. q(β) > 0;
2. β · c > 0 for any class c ∈ NE(X).

Then X is hyperkähler, and β is a Kähler class on X.

Proof. The class β obviously satisfies the first two assumptions of Theorem 4.8.1. Consider the set 𝔚 ⊂ H¹,¹(X) defined as

𝔚 = ⋃_{ξ∈NS(X)\{0\}} (ξ^⊥ ∩ H¹,¹(X)).

The set 𝔚 is a union of a countable number of hyperplanes. If β ∉ 𝔚, then we are done. Assume that β ∈ 𝔚. There is a neighborhood U of β inside H¹,¹(X) such that every β′ ∈ U satisfies the assumptions of the corollary. A very general β′ ∈ U does not lie in 𝔚. Theorem 4.8.1 implies that X is hyperkähler. A class β ∈ H¹,¹(X) on a hyperkähler manifold X is Kähler if and only if it satisfies the two assumptions of the corollary [Bou01, Théorème 1.2] (see also [Huy03, Proposition 3.2]), hence β is a Kähler class. □

# 5. Topology of Shafarevich–Tate twists

In this Section we will prove Theorems B, C and D.

## 5.0.1. Higher pushforwards of \(\mathbb{Q}_X\) do not depend on a twist.
Let \(\pi : X \to B\) be a Lagrangian fibration and \(\pi^{\varphi} : X^{\varphi} \to B\) its Shafarevich–Tate twist. Then the sheaves \(R^k \pi_* \mathbb{Z}\) and \(R^k \pi^{\varphi}_* \mathbb{Z}\) are canonically identified. Indeed, represent \(\varphi\) as a Čech cocycle \((\varphi_{ij})\), where \(\varphi_{ij} \in \operatorname{Aut}^0_{X/B}(U_{ij})\). The automorphisms \(\varphi_{ij}\) are flows of vector fields, hence they act trivially on \(H^k(X_{ij})\).

In particular the vector spaces \(H^0(R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Q})\) and \(H^0(R^2 \pi^{\varphi}_* \mathbb{Q})\) are canonically identified. However, the differentials

\(d_2 : H^0(R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}) \to H^2(R^1 \pi_* \mathbb{Q})\) and \(d_2^{\varphi} : H^0(R^2 \pi^{\varphi}_* \mathbb{Q}) \to H^2(R^1 \pi^{\varphi}_* \mathbb{Q})\)

from the Leray spectral sequence of \(X\) and \(X^{\varphi}\) respectively may be different.

## 5.0.2. The restriction map \(H^2(X) \to H^2(F)\) has rank at most one.
Suppose that \(X\) is hyperkähler, and let \(F\) be a smooth fiber of \(\pi : X \to B\). By Theorem 2.1.6, the restriction map \(H^2(X) \to H^2(F)\) has a one-dimensional image generated by an ample class. Global invariant cycle theorem implies that

\(\operatorname{im}(H^2(X, \mathbb{Q}) \to H^2(F, \mathbb{Q})) = H^2(F, \mathbb{Q})^{\pi_1(B^\circ)} = H^0(B^\circ, R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}|_{B^\circ})\).

Here \(H^2(F, \mathbb{Q})^{\pi_1(B^\circ)}\) denotes the subspace of \(H^2(F)\) invariant under the monodromy action of \(\pi_1(B^\circ)\). It follows that \(H^2(F, \mathbb{Q})^{\pi_1(B^\circ)}\) is one-dimensional and generated by an ample class.

Let \(X^{\varphi}\) be a Shafarevich–Tate twist of \(X\), not necessarily Kähler. Then the image of the map \(H^2(X^{\varphi}, \mathbb{Q}) \to H^2(F, \mathbb{Q})\) still lies in \(H^2(F, \mathbb{Q})^{\pi_1(B^\circ)}\). The latter space is isomorphic to \(H^0(B^\circ, R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}|_{B^\circ})\), hence does not depend on a twist. We obtain the following statement.

### Proposition 5.0.3.
Let \(\pi : X \to B\) be a Lagrangian fibration on an irreducible hyperkähler manifold \(X\) and \(X^{\varphi}\) be its Shafarevich–Tate twist. Then the restriction map

\(H^2(X^{\varphi}) \to H^2(F)\)

is either trivial or has a one-dimensional image generated by an ample class of \(F\).

By 5.0.2 this local system has just one non-trivial section, which is the class of a form of type (1, 1). The class [ξ|_F] is of type (2, 0), hence it must be trivial. There are no non-trivial exact holomorphic forms on F, hence ξ|_F = 0 for every smooth fiber. □

Lemma 5.2.3. Let π : Y → S be a proper Lagrangian fibration over a not necessarily compact base. Consider a holomorphic 2-form ξ on Y with trivial restriction to every smooth fiber. Then ξ induces a map

ι_ξ : π_* T_{Y/S} → Ω_{S}^{[1]}.

Proof. Consider the map

ι_ξ : T_{Y/S} → Ω_Y

sending a vector field v to ι_v ξ. As before, denote by Y⁰ the union of smooth fibers of π and S⁰ := π(Y⁰). For every vertical vector field v, the restriction of ι_v ξ to Y⁰ lies in π^*Ω_{S⁰} because ξ|_F = 0 for every smooth fiber F. It follows that the image of ι_ξ lies in the sheaf (π^*Ω_S)^{sat} consisting of 1-forms α such that α|_{Y⁰} ∈ π^*Ω_{S⁰}. By taking pushforwards, we obtain a map

ι_ξ : π_*T_{Y/S} → π_*(π^*Ω_S^{1})^{sat}.

We will show that (π^*Ω_S)^{sat} ≅ Ω_S^{[1]}. Indeed, this is definitely true over S⁰. Let α be a local section of (π^*Ω_S)^{sat}. Then the restriction of α to Y⁰ is the pullback of a form from S⁰. By Lemma 2.1.10 the form α must be the pullback of a reflexive form from S. □

5.2.4. Let π: X → B be a Lagrangian fibration. Consider the subsheaf (π₍*₎Ω²ₓ)' of π₍*₎Ω²ₓ consisting of holomorphic 2-forms ξ with trivial restriction to all smooth fibers. Thanks to Lemma 5.2.3, there is a natural map

(π₍*₎Ω²ₓ)' → Hom(π₍*₎Tₓ/B, Ω[1]ᴮ).                                      (11)

The holomorphic symplectic form σ on X induces an isomorphism π₍*₎Tₓ/B ≅ Ω[1]ᴮ (Theorem 2.1.11). Composing the map (11) with this isomorphism, we obtain a map of sheaves

ρ: (π₍*₎Ω²ₓ)' → End'ₓ(Ω[1]ᴮ).

Lemma 5.2.5. Define the sheaf End'ₓ(Ω[1]ᴮ) as the image of ρ. Then for any Shafarevich–Tate twist X^φ the sheaf End'ₓφ(Ω[1]ᴮ) coincides with End'ₓ(Ω[1]ᴮ).

Proof. The statement is local on B. For every sufficiently small open disk U ⊂ B, the manifolds π⁻¹(U) and (π^φ)⁻¹(U) are isomorphic as Lagrangian fibrations, hence the claim.

Lemma 5.2.6. The sequence of sheaves on B

0 → Ω[2]ᴮ → (π₍*₎Ω²ₓ)' → End'ₓ(Ω[1]ᴮ) → 0

is exact.

Proof. Note that the map (π₍*₎Ω²ₓ)' → End'ₓ(Ω[1]ᴮ) is surjective by the definition of End'ₓ(Ω[1]ᴮ). The first map Ω[2]ᴮ → (π₍*₎Ω²ₓ)' is clearly injective as well.

The composite map Ω[2]ᴮ → End'ₓ(Ω[1]ᴮ) vanishes. Indeed, let α be a local section of Ω[2]ᴮ. Then for any vertical vector field v, the form ι_v π*α vanishes on X°, hence vanishes everywhere. Therefore, ρ(α) = 0.

It remains to prove exactness in the middle term. Let U ⊂ B be an open subset and ξ a holomorphic 2-form on π⁻¹(U) such that ρ(ξ) = 0. Consider the restriction of ξ to X°. Since ι_v ξ = 0 for every vertical vector field v, the form ξ is contained in π*Ω²ᴮ(π⁻¹(U ∩ B°)). The projection formula together with the fact that π₍*₎𝒪ₓ° ≅ 𝒪ᴮ° implies that

π*Ω²ᴮ(π⁻¹(U ∩ B°)) = π₍*₎π*Ω²ᴮ(U ∩ B°) = Ω²ᴮ(U ∩ B°).

Hence there exists a holomorphic 2-form α° on U ∩ B° such that ξ|₍π⁻¹(U∩B°)₎ = π*α°. By Lemma 2.1.10, ξ = π*α for some reflexive holomorphic 2-form α on U.

We are finally ready to show that all holomorphic 2-forms on X^φ are multiples of σ.

Theorem 5.2.7. Let π: X → B be a Lagrangian fibration on an irreducible hyperkähler manifold X. Then H⁰(Ω²ₓφ) is generated by the holomorphic symplectic form σ for all φ ∈ Ш.

Proof. By Lemma 5.2.2 every holomorphic 2-form ξ on X^φ restricts trivially to every smooth fiber. Therefore,

H⁰(B, (π₍*₎Ω²ₓφ)') = H⁰(X^φ, Ω²ₓφ).

Lemma 5.2.6 shows that the sequence

0 → Ω[2]ᴮ → (π₍*₎Ω²ₓφ)' → End'ₓφ(Ω[1]ᴮ) → 0

is exact. Consider its long exact sequence of cohomology

0 → H⁰(Ω[2]ᴮ) → H²,⁰(X^φ) → H⁰(End'ₓφ(Ω[1]ᴮ)) → H¹(Ω[2]ᴮ)

The cohomology groups Hⁱ(Ω[2]ᴮ) vanish for i = 0,1 (Theorem 2.1.16). Therefore,

H²,⁰(X^φ) ≅ H⁰(End'ₓφ(Ω[1]ᴮ)).

The sheaf End'ₓφ(Ω[1]ᴮ) does not depend on a twist by Lemma 5.2.5, therefore H²,⁰(X^φ) does not depend on a twist.

Remark 5.2.8. When \( B = \mathbb{P}^n \) the proof of Theorem 5.2.7 can be simplified because \(\mathrm{End}(\Omega^1_{\mathbb{P}^n}) \simeq \mathbb{C}\). By Lemma 5.2.2, every holomorphic 2-form \(\xi\) on \(X^\varphi\) restricts trivially to smooth fibers. Hence \(\xi\) induces an endomorphism \(\rho(\xi)\) of \(\Omega^1_{\mathbb{P}^n}\) (Lemma 5.2.3). Since \(\mathrm{End}(\Omega^1_{\mathbb{P}^n}) = \mathbb{C}\), there exists a number \(\lambda \in \mathbb{C}\) such that \(\rho(\xi - \lambda \sigma) = 0\). The contraction of every vertical vector field on \(X\) with \(\xi - \lambda \sigma\) vanishes, hence

\[
(\xi - \lambda \sigma)|_{X^\circ} \in \pi^* \Omega^2_{(\mathbb{P}^n)^\circ}.
\]

Since \(\pi_* \pi^* \Omega^2_{\mathbb{P}^n} = \Omega^2_{\mathbb{P}^n}\), we have \((\xi - \lambda \sigma)|_{X^\circ} = \pi^* \alpha^\circ\) for some holomorphic 2-form \(\alpha^\circ\) on \((\mathbb{P}^n)^\circ\). By Lemma 2.1.10, \(\alpha^\circ\) extends to a holomorphic form \(\alpha\) on \(\mathbb{P}^n\) and \(\xi - \lambda \sigma = \pi^* \alpha\). There are no holomorphic forms on \(\mathbb{P}^n\), hence \(\xi = \lambda \sigma\).

We were unable to show that \(\mathrm{End}(\Omega^{[1]}_B) \simeq \mathbb{C}\) for any base of a Lagrangian fibration, although we expect it to be true.

5.2.9. Proof of Theorem B. The statement immediately follows from Proposition 5.1.2 and Theorem 5.2.7.

## 5.3 Second cohomology of a twist

Our goal now is to prove Theorem C and Theorem D.

Lemma 5.3.1. Let \(\pi\colon X \to B\) be a Lagrangian fibration on an irreducible hyperkähler manifold \(X\). Define the sheaf \(\mathrm{NS}\) on \(B\) as the image of the Chern class map \(R^1 \pi_* \mathcal{O}_X^\times \to R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Z}\). Then

\[
H^0(B, \mathrm{NS}) = H^0(R^2\pi_* \mathbb{Z}).
\]

In other words, for every section \(\xi\) of \(R^2\pi_* \mathbb{Z}\) and a sufficiently fine open cover \(B = \bigcup U_i\), there are line bundles \(L_i\) on \(X_i\) such that \(\xi|_{U_i} = c_1(L_i)\). In particular, every section \(\xi\) of \(R^1\pi_* \mathbb{Z}\) is locally the class of a closed (1,1)-form.

Proof. Consider the exponential exact sequence

\[
0 \to \mathbb{Z}_X \to \mathcal{O}_X \to \mathcal{O}_X^\times \to 0.
\]

It induces a long exact sequence of pushforward sheaves:

\[
R^1 \pi_* \mathcal{O}_X^\times \to R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Z} \to R^2 \pi_* \mathcal{O}_X.
\]

The sheaf \(R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Z} / \mathrm{im}(R^1\pi_* \mathcal{O}_X^\times) = R^2\pi_* \mathbb{Z} / \mathrm{NS}\) is a subsheaf of \(R^2 \pi_* \mathcal{O}_X \simeq \Omega^{[2]}_B\). Since \(H^0(\Omega^2_B) = 0\) (Theorem 2.1.16), the sheaf \(R^2\pi_* \mathbb{Z} / \mathrm{NS}\) has no global sections. Hence the natural inclusion \(H^0(B, \mathrm{NS}) \to H^0(R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Z})\) is an isomorphism.

5.3.2. Isomorphisms between \(T_{X/B}\) and \(R^1\pi_* \mathcal{O}_X\). Let \(\xi\) be a global section of \(H^0(R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Q})\). It defines a map \(f_\xi\colon \pi_* T_{X/B} \to R^1 \pi_* \mathcal{O}_X\) in a similar way that a class \(\omega \in H^2(X, \mathbb{Q})\) defines a map \(f_\omega\) in 2.1.12. Namely, by Lemma 5.3.1 we can represent \(\xi|_{U_i}\) by a closed (1,1)–form \(\xi_i\) on \(X_i\). Consider the map

\[
f_{\xi_i} \colon \pi_* T_{X_i/U_i} \to R^1\pi_* \mathcal{O}_X|_{U_i}
\]

sending \(v\) to the class of \([\iota_v \xi_i]\) under the \(\bar{\partial}\)-differential. Since the sheaves \(\pi_* T_{X/B}\) and \(R^1 \pi_* \mathcal{O}_X\) are torsion-free (Theorem 2.1.13), the map \(f_{\xi_i}\) is determined completely by its restriction to \(B^\circ \cap U_i\). Therefore, the map \(f_{\xi_i}\) depends only on the class \([\xi|_F]\) of the restriction of \(\xi\) to smooth fibers. In particular, maps \(f_{\xi_i}\) do not depend on the choice of \(\xi_i\) representing \(\xi \in H^0(R^2\pi_* \mathbb{Q})\) and glue into a map

\[
f_\xi : \pi_* T_{X/B} \to R^1 \pi_* \mathcal{O}_X.
\]

5.3.3. The map \(H^0(B, R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{C}) \to \mathrm{Hom}(\pi_* T_{X/B}, R^1 \pi_* \mathcal{O}_X)\) sending \(\xi\) to \(f_\xi\) factors through the restriction to a smooth fiber \(F\):

\[
H^0(B, R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{C}) \to H^0(B^\circ, R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{C}) = H^2(F)^{\pi_1(B^\circ)} \to \mathrm{Hom}(\pi_* T_{X/B}, R^1 \pi_* \mathcal{O}_X).
\]

The vector space \( H^2(F)^{\pi_1(B^\circ)} \) is one-dimensional by 5.0.2. Fix an element \( \xi_0 \in H^0(B, R^2\pi_* \mathbb{Z}) \) which restricts non-trivially to \( F \) and let \( f_0 := f_{\xi_0} \) be the induced isomorphism \( \pi_* T_{X/B} \to R^1 \pi_* \mathcal{O}_X \). For every \( \xi \in H^0(B, R^2\pi_* \mathbb{C}) \) we define a number \( \lambda_\xi \) by the identity

\[ f_\xi = \lambda_\xi f_0. \]

Recall that the isomorphism \( f_0 : \pi_* T_{X/B} \to R^1 \pi_* \mathcal{O}_X \) sends \( \Gamma_{\mathbb{Q}} = \Gamma \otimes \mathbb{Q} \subset \pi_* T_{X/B} \) isomorphically onto \( R^1 \pi_* \mathbb{Q} \subset R^1 \pi_* \mathcal{O}_X \) (2.2.5). We identify the group \( H^2(\Gamma_{\mathbb{Q}}) = (\amalg / \amalg^0) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \) with \( H^2(R^1 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}) \) using the isomorphism \( f_0 \).

### 5.3.4. Boundary map \( \amalg \to H^2(\Gamma) \)

The boundary map \( \amalg \to H^2(\Gamma) \) can be described in terms of Čech cocycles as follows. Pick \( \varphi \in \amalg \) and represent it by a 1-cocycle \( \varphi_{ij} \in Aut^0_{X/B}(U_{ij}) \). We can find a vertical vector field \( v_{ij} \) on \( X_{ij} \) such that \( \exp(v_{ij}) = \varphi_{ij} \). The vector field \( v_{ij} + v_{jk} + v_{ki} \) on \( X_{ijk} \) lies in \( \Gamma \) and represents the class \( \overline{\varphi} \in H^2(\Gamma) \).

### 5.3.5. Boundary map \( H^0(R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}) \to H^2(R^1 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}) \)

We will describe the boundary map

\[ d_2 : H^0(R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}) \to H^2(R^1 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}) \cong H^2(\Gamma_{\mathbb{Q}}) \]

from the Leray spectral sequence of \( \pi \) in terms of Čech cocycles. Let \( \xi \) be a section of \( H^0(R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}) \). Represent it locally by (1,1)-forms \( \xi_i \) on \( X_i \). The difference \( \xi_j - \xi_i \) is an exact form, hence

\[ \xi_j - \xi_i = d\rho_{ij} \]

for some 1-form \( \rho_{ij} \) on \( X_{ij} \). The form \( \rho_{ij} + \rho_{jk} + \rho_{ki} \) is closed on \( X_{ijk} \), hence defines a cocycle with coefficients in \( R^1 \pi_* \mathbb{Q} \). By 2.2.5 there exists a unique vertical vector field \( w_{ijk} \in \Gamma_{\mathbb{Q}}(U_{ijk}) \) on \( X_{ijk} \) such that \( f_0(w_{ijk}) \) is equal to the class of the (0,1)-form \( \rho_{ij}^{0,1} + \rho_{jk}^{0,1} + \rho_{ki}^{0,1} \) under the \( \bar{\partial} \)-differential.

### Proposition 5.3.6. 
Let \( \pi: X \to B \) be a Lagrangian fibration. Pick a class \( \varphi \in \amalg \). Let

\[ d_2^\varphi : H^0(R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}) \to H^2(R^1 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}) \cong H^2(\Gamma_{\mathbb{Q}}) \]

be the differential in the Leray spectral sequence for \( \pi^\varphi : X^\varphi \to B \). Then for any \( \xi \in H^0(R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}) \)

\[ d_2^\varphi(\xi) = d_2(\xi) + \lambda_\xi \overline{\varphi}. \]

Proof. Represent \( \xi \in H^0(R^2 \pi_* \mathbb{Q}) \) by a collection of closed (1,1)-forms \( \xi_i \) on \( X_i \). When we view \( X_i \) as an open subset of \( X^\varphi \), we will denote the same forms by \( \xi_i^\varphi \). The difference \( \xi_j^\varphi - \xi_i^\varphi \) is not the same as \( \xi_j - \xi_i \) because there is a twist by \( \varphi_{ij} \in Aut^0_{X/B}(U_{ij}) \) involved. Namely

\[ \xi_j^\varphi - \xi_i^\varphi = \varphi_{ij}^* \xi_j - \xi_i = (\varphi_{ij}^* \xi_j - \xi_j) + (\xi_j - \xi_i). \]

As in 5.3.5, write \( \xi_j - \xi_i = d\rho_{ij} \). Find a vector field \( v_{ij} \) such that \( \varphi_{ij} = \exp(v_{ij}) \). Then

\[
\begin{align*}
\varphi_{ij}^* \xi_j - \xi_j &= \int_0^1 \frac{d}{dt} \exp(tv_{ij})^* \xi_j dt \\
&= \int_0^1 \exp(tv_{ij})^* L_{v_{ij}} \xi_j dt \\
&= \int_0^1 \exp(tv_{ij})^* (d \iota_{v_{ij}} + \iota_{v_{ij}} d)\xi_j dt \\
&= d \int_0^1 \exp(tv_{ij})^* \iota_{v_{ij}} \xi_j dt.
\end{align*}
\]

Here \( L \) denotes the Lie derivative. Set \( \gamma_{ij} = \int_0^1 \exp(tv_{ij})^* \iota_{v_{ij}} \xi_j dt \). Then

\[ \xi_j^\varphi - \xi_i^\varphi = d(\rho_{ij} + \gamma_{ij}). \]

The form \( \gamma_{ij} \) is of type (0,1) and \( \bar{\partial} \)-closed. Its class under the \( \bar{\partial} \)-differential is

\[ \int_0^1 [\exp(tv_{ij})^* \iota_{v_{ij}} \xi_j ] dt = \int_0^1 [\iota_{v_{ij}}\xi_j] dt = [\iota_{v_{ij}}\xi_j] = f_\xi(v_{ij}) = \lambda_\xi f_0(v_{ij}). \]

As in 5.3.5, let \( w_{ijk} \) be the element in \( \Gamma_{Q}(U_{ijk}) \) such that \( f_{0}(w_{ijk}) = [\rho_{ij}^{0,1} + \rho_{jk}^{0,1} + \rho_{ki}^{0,1}] \). It represents \( d_{2}(\xi) \). Then \( d_{2}^{\varphi}(\xi) - d_{2}(\xi) \) can be represented by the cocycle
\[
\lambda_{\xi}(v_{ij} + v_{jk} + v_{ki}).
\]
By 5.3.4 the class of the cocycle \( v_{ij} + v_{jk} + v_{ki} \) in Čech cohomology is \( \overline{\varphi} \), hence the claim.

Corollary 5.3.7. Let \( \pi\colon X \to B \) be a Lagrangian fibration. Then for any \( \varphi \in \amalg' \) we have \( d_{2}^{\varphi} = d_{2} \).

Proof. For any \( \varphi \in \amalg' \), we have \( \overline{\varphi} = 0 \). Proposition 5.3.6 implies that \( d_{2}^{\varphi} = d_{2} \).

Corollary 5.3.8. Let \( \pi\colon X \to B \) be a Lagrangian fibration. Pick a class \( \varphi \in \amalg \) such that \( \overline{\varphi} \neq 0 \). Consider the restriction maps
\[
r\colon H^{2}(X) \to H^{0}(R^{2}\pi_{*}\mathbb{Q}), \quad r^{\varphi}\colon H^{2}(X^{\varphi}) \to H^{0}(R^{2}\pi_{*}\mathbb{Q}).
\]
Let \( H^{2}(X)^{0} \) (resp. \( H^{2}(X^{\varphi})^{0} \)) denote the subspace of classes in \( H^{2}(X) \) (resp. \( H^{2}(X^{\varphi}) \)) that restrict trivially to a smooth fiber. Then
\[
\mathrm{im}\, r \cap \mathrm{im}\, r^{\varphi} = r(H^{2}(X)^{0}) = r^{\varphi}(H^{2}(X^{\varphi})^{0}).
\]

Proof. The image of the restriction map \( r \) (resp. \( r^{\varphi} \)) coincides with the kernel of \( d_{2} \) (resp. \( d_{2}^{\varphi} \)). By Proposition 5.3.6 a class \( \xi \) lies in the kernel of both \( d_{2} \) and \( d_{2}^{\varphi} \) if and only if \( \lambda_{\xi} = 0 \), i.e., the restriction of \( \xi \) to a smooth fiber is trivial. The claim follows.

Proposition 5.3.9. Let \( \pi\colon X \to B \) be a Lagrangian fibration on an irreducible hyperkähler manifold and \( X^{\varphi} \) its Shafarevich–Tate twist. Then either \( b_{2}(X^{\varphi}) = b_{2}(X) \) or \( b_{2}(X^{\varphi}) = b_{2}(X) - 1 \). The first case occurs if and only if there is a class \( h \in H^{2}(X) \) whose restriction to a smooth fiber is non-trivial.

Proof. It follows from the Leray spectral sequence for \( \mathbb{Q}_{X^{\varphi}} \) that
\[
b_{2}(X^{\varphi}) = b_{2}(B) + \dim H^{1}(R^{1}\pi_{*} \mathbb{Q}) + \mathrm{rk}\, r^{\varphi}. \tag{12}
\]
Indeed,
\[
h^{2}(X^{\varphi}) = E^{2,0}_{\infty} + E^{1,1}_{\infty} + E^{0,2}_{\infty}.
\]
The vector space \( H^{0}(R^{1}\pi_{*}\mathbb{Q}) \) vanishes by Proposition 5.1.2, hence the map \( H^{2}(B) \to H^{2}(X^{\varphi}) \) is injective and \( E^{2,0}_{\infty} = E^{2,0}_{2} \). Moreover, the second differential \( d_{2}\colon H^{1}(R^{1}\pi_{*}\mathbb{Q}) \to H^{3}(B, \mathbb{Q}) \) is zero because \( H^{3}(B, \mathbb{Q}) = 0 \) [SY22]. Thus \( E^{1,1}_{\infty} = E^{1,1}_{2} \). The formula (12) implies that
\[
b_{2}(X^{\varphi}) - b_{2}(X) = \mathrm{rk}\, r^{\varphi} - \mathrm{rk}\, r.
\]
Since \( X \) is hyperkähler, the subspace \( H^{2}(X)^{0} \) has codimension 1 in \( H^{2}(X) \), hence
\[
\mathrm{rk}\, r = \dim r(H^{2}(X)^{0}) + 1 = \dim r^{\varphi}(H^{2}(X^{\varphi})^{0}) + 1,
\]
where the last equality holds by Corollary 5.3.7 if \( \overline{\varphi} = 0 \) and by Corollary 5.3.8 if \( \overline{\varphi} \neq 0 \). If there is a class in \( H^{2}(X^{\varphi}) \) restricting non-trivially to a smooth fiber, then \( H^{2}(X^{\varphi})^{0} \) is a hyperplane in \( H^{2}(X^{\varphi}) \) (Proposition 5.0.3) and
\[
\mathrm{rk}\, r^{\varphi} = \dim r^{\varphi}(H^{2}(X^{\varphi})^{0}) + 1 = \mathrm{rk}\, r.
\]
In this case, \( b_{2}(X) = b_{2}(X^{\varphi}) \). Otherwise, \( H^{2}(X^{\varphi})^{0} = H^{2}(X^{\varphi}) \), hence
\[
\mathrm{rk}\, r^{\varphi} = \dim r^{\varphi}(H^{2}(X^{\varphi})^{0}) = \mathrm{rk}\, r - 1,
\]
and
\[
b_{2}(X) = b_{2}(X^{\varphi}) + 1.
\]

Proposition 5.3.10. Let \( \pi\colon X \to B \) be a Lagrangian fibration on an irreducible hyperkähler manifold and \( X^{\varphi} \) its Shafarevich–Tate twist. Then there is a class \( h \in H^{2}(X^{\varphi}) \) that restricts non-trivially to a smooth fiber if and only if \( \varphi \in H^{2}(\Gamma_{\mathbb{Q}}) \) is in the image of the boundary map \( d_{2}\colon H^{0}(R^{2}\pi_{*}\mathbb{Q}) \to H^{2}(R^{1}\pi_{*}\mathbb{Q}) \cong H^{2}(\Gamma_{\mathbb{Q}}) \).

Proof. Suppose that there is a class \( h \in H^2(X^\varphi) \) that restricts non-trivially to a smooth fiber. Let \( \bar{h} \) be its image in \( H^0(B, R^2\pi_*\mathbb{Q}) \). By Proposition 5.3.6

\[
0 = d_2^\varphi(\bar{h}) = d_2(\bar{h}) + \lambda_h \overline{\varphi}.
\]

Therefore, \( \overline{\varphi} = -d_2(\bar{h}) / \lambda_h \) is in the image of \( d_2 \).

Conversely, suppose that \( \overline{\varphi} \) is in the image of \( d_2 \), i.e., there is a class \( \xi \in H^0(R^2\pi_*\mathbb{Q}) \) such that \( d_2\xi = \overline{\varphi} \). Let \( h_0 \) be a class on \( X \) with \( \lambda_{h_0} = 1 \) and \( \bar{h_0} \) its image in \( H^0(R^2\pi_*\mathbb{Q}) \). Consider the class

\[
\xi' := (1 + \lambda_\xi)\bar{h_0} - \xi.
\]

Then \( \lambda_{\xi'} = 1 \) and

\[
d_2^\varphi(\xi') = d_2(\xi') + \overline{\varphi} = (1 + \lambda_\xi)d_2(\bar{h_0}) - d_2(\xi) + \overline{\varphi} = -d_2(\xi) + \overline{\varphi} = 0.
\]

Here the first equality holds by Proposition 5.3.6. Therefore, \( \xi' \) lifts to a class in \( H^2(X^\varphi) \), which restricts non-trivially to smooth fibers.

5.3.11. Proof of Theorem C. Immediately follows from Propositions 5.3.9 and 5.3.10.

5.3.12. Proof of Theorem D. Suppose \( X^{\varphi} \) is of Fujiki class \( \mathcal{C} \), i.e., there is a rational map \( f : X^{\varphi} \dashrightarrow Y \) to a Kähler manifold \( Y \). Let \( h \in H^2(X, \mathbb{R}) \) be the pullback of a Kähler form on \( Y \). The restriction of \( f \) to a general fiber \( F \) of \( \pi^\varphi \) is birational onto its image, hence \( h|_F \) is non-trivial. By Theorem C, the twist satisfies \( \overline{\varphi} \in \mathrm{im}\, d_2 \).

Remark 5.3.13. Consider an abelian surface \( A \) which is a product of elliptic curves \( A = E \times F \). Let \( K^n(A) \) be the generalized Kummer variety of \( A \). It admits a Lagrangian fibration \( \pi: K^n(A) \to \mathbb{P}^n \) whose general fiber is isomorphic to \( F^n \). Let \( p: S \to E \) be a primary Kodaira surface which is a principal torsor over \( F \). This is a non-Kähler holomorphic symplectic surface. Consider its associated Bogomolov–Guan manifold \( BG^n(S) \) [Gua95, Bog96]. It admits a Lagrangian fibration \( \pi': BG^n(S) \to \mathbb{P}^n \) whose general fiber is also isomorphic to \( F^n \). Actually the non-Kähler holomorphic symplectic manifold \( BG^n(S) \) is a Shafarevich–Tate twist of \( K^n(A) \). As computed in [Gua95, Theorem 2], \( b_2(BG^n(S)) = 6 \), which is exactly \( b_2(K^n(A)) - 1 \) in accordance with Theorem C. Theorem C also shows that the rank of the restriction map \( H^2(BG^n(A)) \to H^2(F^n) \) is zero.
