Abstract Sedentary behavior for two years during the coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) pandemic contributes to weight gain. Gut microbiota and blood metabolome are related to body mass index (BMI) and indicate individual metabolic changes. Surgery and exercise are effective weight-loss methods. The precise plasma metabolites and gut microbiota biomarkers involved and the underlying mechanisms are still largely unclear. To address this issue, we analyzed weight gain and weight loss cohorts to identify biomarkers associated with obesity. In the sedentary cohort, 49 subjects were recruited in year 2019. After two years of sedentary behavior during the COVID-19 pandemic, the BMI of 24 subjects significantly increased (Weight gain group), while that of the remnant 25 subjects remained constant (Maintaining weight group). At baseline and two years post baseline, the gut microbiota and blood metabolome, as well as body composition and clinical indicators, were all collected. In weight loss studies, we analyze the plasma metabolome of the two cohorts, including individuals who underwent laparoscopic sleeve gastrectomy (LSG) surgery and exercise intervention. Weight gain through sedentary behavior contributed to the variation of the gut microbiota and plasma metabolites composition. Creatine, phenylalanine and tyrosine exhibited significant positive associations with BMI and fat mass. We further confirmed the association between BMI and plasma metabolites in two weight loss cohorts. By utilizing a linear regression model, we found that 10 metabolites including creatine were correlated with BMI in weight loss individuals. Based on receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves, creatine exhibited a satisfactory classification performance in regard to predicting weight reduction (AUC LSG = 0.890, AUC S ports = 0.840). Moreover, some gut microbiota, including Bifidobacterium angulatum DSM 20098 = JCM 7096 and Rothia dentocariosa M567I could affect BMI through the mediating factor of creatine. Keywords: Obesity, Weight gain, Weight loss, Metabolomics, Metagenome, Biomarkers Introduction The coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) pandemic profoundly altered individuals’ lifestyle and led to physical inactivity (Pellegrini et al. 2020 ; Vaz et al. 2022 ). Sedentary behavior is among the leading modifiable risk factors for obesity, metabolic disorder and all-cause mortality (Lavie et al. 2019 ). Studies have revealed that sedentary time, a circulating metabolic marker, is related to the risk of metabolic diseases (Bao et al. 2021 ; Pang et al. 2019 ), however, the application of multi-omics approaches for delineating the harm occasioned by sedentary behavior, as well as the correlations with phenotypes remains to be investigated. Gut microbiota is closely connected with individuals’ metabolic state (Yao et al. 2023 ). The crosstalk between gut microbiota and the circulating system and as well as other organs profoundly influenced the whole-body homeostasis (Schroeder and Bäckhed 2016 ). Human blood metabolome identified by targeted metabolomic analyses provides an in-depth characterization of small circulating molecules for overweightness (Chen et al. 2023 ; Johnson et al. 2016 ; Newgard 2017 ; Wu et al. 2021 ). Sedentary behavior leads to metabolic dysregulation, and alters gut microbiota and plasma metabolomic fingerprints, leading to resulting in impaired insulin sensitivity and glucose uptake (Morville et al. 2020 ; Thyfault and Bergouignan 2020 ). By addressing obesity through weight-loss interventions, researchers can attenuate comorbidities and enhance quality of life (Liu et al. 2021 ; Tschöp et al. 2016 ). Exercise and bariatric surgery have exhibited effectiveness in sustaining weight loss, enhancing insulin sensitivity and integrating metabolic health (Pillon et al. 2022 ; Sandoval and Patti 2023 ). Herein, we addressed the signatures of gut microbiota and plasma metabolites between weight-gain and unchanged groups after two years of sedentary behavior, and we also analyzed the correlated between these gut microbiotas and plasma metabolites. The variance in the gut microbiota and plasma metabolites is reflected in the host phenotypes, including body mass index (BMI), hemoglobin A1C (HbA1c) and fat mass. More crucially, these microbiotas and metabolites may serve as predictive cues for the resistance to weight gain occasioned by sedentary behavior. In addition, we described the fingerprints of plasma metabolites after different weight-loss interventions. Moreover, we further identified that the plasma level of creatine exhibited consistency in three weight-changed cohorts, the level of which is positively correlated with BMI, and creatine exhibits more optimal predictive accuracy in diagnosing weight loss in laparoscopic sleeve gastrectomy (LSG) and exercise interventions. The findings indicated that creatine was a potential treatment for obesity. Methods Participant Recruitment Study 1: Sedentary Cohort In the sedentary cohort, subjects were recruited at year 2019 before the COVID-19 pandemic. After the two-year lock-down, all of the subjects remained uninfected; however, they experienced long term physical inactivity and sedentary behavior. A uniform diet, provided by the university in compliance with the guidelines, was offered (Gao et al. 2021 ). Infected individuals were excluded to rule out the secondary effect of COVID-19. Moreover, individuals with chronic diseases were excluded. Additionally, individuals dieting, the consumption of dietary supplements, a history of eating disorders or the utilization of daily medications were excluded for participation. Subjects with complete clinical data at baseline and follow-up were included. Finally, 49 subjects were enrolled. All subjects provided written informed consent, and the study protocol was approved by the Ethics Committee of the Shanghai University of Sport (NO. 2018047). Study 2: LSG Cohort Subjects were recruited at the Department of General Surgery, Huashan Hospital, Fudan University. All of the subjects had obesity diagnoses and were suited for LSG surgery. An identical standardized operation was performed. The longitudinal resection of the stomach from the angle of His to approximately 5 cm proximal to the pylorus was performed using a 36-Frbougie inserted along the lesser curvature (Yuan et al. 2012 ). Baseline clinical data and follow-up clinical data were both obtained. Individuals with chronic diseases, a history of alcohol abuse or smoking were excluded. Additionally, factors including individuals dieting, the consumption of dietary supplements, a history of eating disorders or utilization of daily medications were excluded for participation. Finally, 38 patients who were aged between 19- and 61 years old and exhibited no medical conditions were enrolled. All subjects provided written informed consent, and the study protocol was approved by the Huashan Hospital Ethical Committee of Fudan University (NO. 2020727). Study 3: Exercise Intervention (Sports) Cohort Only subjects enrolled in a weight-loss summer camps program were recruited. All participants participated in a four-week high-intensity exercise intervention as previously described (Lin et al. 2023 ). According to the criteria (Katzmarzyk et al. 2020 ), only participants who lost at least 3 kg during the four-week exercise intervention were considered for this study (Table 3 ). Individuals with chronic diseases were excluded. Additionally, individuals dieting, the consumption of dietary supplements, a history of eating disorders or utilization of daily medications were excluded for participation. Subjects with complete clinical data at baseline and after exercise intervention were included. Finally, 20 patients aged between 9- and 16 years old and without any medical conditions were enrolled. All subjects provided written informed consent, thus indicating that parental permissions were provided for all the procedures, and the study protocol was approved by the Ethical Committee of the Shanghai University of Sport (NO. 102772021RT112). Chromatographic separations were performed on an ACQUITY BEH C18 column (1.7 μm, 100 mm × 2.l mm internal dimensions (Waters, Milford, MA). The instrument was operated in positive (POS) and negative (NEG) ion modes. A total of 306 standards were sourced from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA), Steraloids (Newport, RI, USA), and TRC Chemicals (Toronto, ON, Canada). Each standard was accurately weighed and prepared in a suitable solution to generate individual stock solutions at a concentration of 5.0 mg·mL −1 . These stock solutions were subsequently combined in appropriate proportions to create stock calibration solutions. Plamsa samples were processed and analyzed following established methods (Gou et al. 2021 ; Jiang et al. 2020 ). In brief, 25 μL of plasma was aliquoted into a 96-well plate. Subsequently, 120 μL of ice-cold methanol with internal standards was automatically added to each sample, followed by vortexing for 5 min. After centrifugation at 4000 × g for 30 min, 30 μL of the supernatant was transferred to a clean 96-well plate. To each well, 20 μL of derivative reagents was added, and the derivatization process was performed at 30 °C for 60 min. Following derivatization, 330 μL of a 50% methanol solution was added, and the plate was stored at− 20 °C for 20 min. After centrifugation at 4000 × g at 4 °C for 30 min, the obtained supernatant was utilized for liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC–MS) analysis. In the LSG cohort and Exercise intervention cohort study, the metabolomic methodology was adapted from a previously published procedure (Zhang et al. 2019 ). In brief, samples were reconstituted in 50 μl of a water: acetonitrile mixture (v:v, 50:50), and 5 μl of the solution was injected into a 6500 QTRAP triple-quadrupole MS (SCIEX) coupled with an HPLC system (Shimadzu). Metabolites were separated through hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC) using a 4.6 mm inner diameter × 10 cm Amide XBridge column (Waters) with a flow rate of 400 μl/min utilizing buffer A (20 mM ammonium hydroxide/20 mM ammonium acetate, pH 9.2, in a 95:5 ratio with water: acetonitrile) and buffer B (acetonitrile). Gradient elution was performed from 85% buffer B to 42% buffer B at 0–5 min, from 42% buffer B to 0% buffer B at 5–16 min, holding at 0% buffer B from 16–24 min, shifting from 0% buffer B to 85% buffer B at 24–25 min, and holding at 85% buffer B for 7 min. A total of 306 selected reaction monitoring transitions were acquired for all ions in a positive and negative mode switching manner. Electrospray ionization (ESI) voltage was set at + 4,900 and− 4500 V in positive and negative modes, respectively. Fecal Microbial Profiling Stool samples were collected and initially stored at − 20 °C for a maximum of 24 h before further preservation at− 80 °C. Stool DNA extraction was performed according to the Metagenomics of the Human Intestinal Tract (MetaHIT) protocol, as detailed in previous studies (Qin et al. 2012 ). In summary, 500 ng of stool DNA was employed for library construction, and the subsequent sequencing involved paired-end reads of 150 bp on the MGISEQ-2000 platform. The taxonomic composition was obtained by utilizing an updated version of the MEDUSA pipeline (Karlsson et al. 2014 ), in which the raw reads were trimmed by FASTX ( http://hannonlab.cshl.edu/fastx_toolkit/ ; with a quality threshold of 20 bp and minimum length of 35 bp), filtered to remove human reads, and subsequently mapped to bacterial gene and genome catalogs using Bowtie2. An additional filter was applied during the mapping process containing reads with at least 95% identity to obtain high-quality reads. Only strains present in at least 80% of samples were utilized for analysis. The meta omic analysis toolkit 2 (MOCAT2) was used to generate a non-redundant assembly of microbial genes. Basic local alignment search tool for nucleotide sequences (BLASTN) and Basic local alignment search tool translated nucleotide database using a protein query (BLASTX) were employed for classification and functional annotation of the newly assembled genes against the National center of biotechnology information (. Clinical Characterization Subjects selected for the study underwent baseline and follow-up clinical test and plasma sampling. Subjects fasted for 10–12 h overnight prior to participation. Blood samples were collected in the fasting state. The percentage of body fat is presented as measured by dual x-ray absorptiometry (DXA). Statistical Analysis All statistical and modeling analyses were performed in the R environment (Team 2015 ). Data are presented as mean (Standard Error). The principal coordinates analysis on the Bray–Curtis dissimilarity matrix was performed. All these functions are in the vegan package (Oksanen 2015 ). Two-tailed Mann–Whitney U test was utilized to determine significance in unpaired groups, and two-tailed Wilcoxon matched-pairs signed rank test was used in the follow-up samples. Log-transformed data were tested and significant metabolites with p &lt; 0.05, were derived. Pairwise statistical test, pearson correlation analysis, was performed using the R packages psych (version 2.2.9). Partial least-square discriminant analysis (PLS-DA) was performed on the metabolic data using the R package “mixOmics” to obtain the Variable important in projection (VIP) score. We utilized R package “ggplot2” for drawing display. The receiver operating characteristic curves was calculate using R package “pROC” and “glmnet”. Results Participant Characteristics To investigate the impact of sedentary behavior, we included 49 subjects and performed a two-year follow-up study. Briefly, a total of 49 participants were recruited. Participants were divided into two groups according to the body weight change in the follow-up study. Table 1 describes the clinical characteristics of the baseline and follow-up study. After two years of sedentary behavior during the COVID-19 pandemic, the weight of 24 subjects significantly increased (Weight gain, 18–20 years old), whereas that of the remnant 25 subjects remained constant (Maintaining weight, 18–22 years old). Additionally, there was a largely significant increase in the weight gain group’s clinical characteristics such as HbA1c, Total fat mass (FM), and Total-Lean mass. However, in the maintaining group, there was only a significant increase in HbA1c. Particularly, HbA1c was regarded as the gold standard for predicting glycaemia-associated risks for the microvascular and macrovascular complications of diabetes mellitus over 5–10 years. Open in a new tab The ROC showed the performance of the base model and the metabolite models in the LSG and Exercise intervention cohort Discussion This study identified the signatures of gut microbiota and plasma metabolites after sedentary behavior and analyzed the correlation together with the mediating effect. Weight loss cohorts further confirmed the associations between plasma metabolites and BMI. Receiver operating characteristic curves indicated that out of the BMI-correlated metabolites, creatine promoted the most optimal classification performance in regard to predicting weight reduction. Studies have reported that higher plasma creatine is associated with an increased risk of obesity (Post et al. 2021 ) and type 2 diabetes in males (Post et al. 2021 ). This study enriched the understanding on the relationship between creatine and BMI. First, we found that creatine was not only associated with BMI but also related to Total FM. Second, mediation analysis further exhibited that a causal link mediated by creatine might exist between some bacteria ( Bifidobacterium angulatum DSM 20098 = JCM 7096 and Rothia dentocariosa M567I ) and BMI. Gut microbiota were sensitive to metabolism and lifestyle. The α-diversity of gut microbiota may profile the classification for the BMI condition (Scheiman et al. 2019 ). The composition of gut microbiota also varied in aging (Lynch and Pedersen 2016 ) and glycemic-impaired individuals (Wu et al. 2020 ). Thus, we compared the individuals with the similar age, BMI and glycemic status before sedentary behavior and grouped them according to BMI responses, thereby minimizing the secondary effect of age, body weight and insulin sensitivity difference. Third, creatine exhibited a predictive effective for BMI. The prediction model indicated that creatine promoted the most optimal classification performance in regard to predicting weight reduction out of compared to the BMI-correlated metabolites. Different weight-loss approaches would identically shape the metabolic signatures. Ben-Zvi’s study indicated that sleeve gastrectomy primarily influenced one-carbon metabolic pathways and downregulated the liver methionine cycle, whereas weight-loss through caloric restriction did not significantly affect the methionine cycle (Wilmanski et al. 2019 ). The metabolic changes caused by different weight loss methods may be also different. But either exercise or surgical intervention shrank the plasma creatine level during weight loss, which was beneficial for glycemic control. When individuals became fatter, the creatine levels increased in the sedentary cohort. By revealing the link between gut microbiota and plasma metabolome in the obese individuals as well as two weight loss cohorts, we observed that creatine is a treatment-independent biomarker, that exhibits attractiveness as a mediating factor and predictive cue for obesity. Conclusion This study identified the gut microbiota and plasma metabolite signatures after sedentary behavior. Both the weight gain and weight loss cohorts confirmed the association between creatine and BMI. Using a mediation analysis, the following was revealed: a creatine-mediated causal link might exist between gut microbiota and BMI. Creatine was related to weight gain caused by sedentary behavior, possibly due to gut microbiota. What’s more, among all the detected plasma metabolites, creatine could serve as a metabolite marker for predicting weight change.
