Introduction Feeding is vital for animals and humans to obtain essential energy and nutrients, exerting profound effects on body weight, energy homeostasis, immune function, and even lifespan [ 1 ]. The CNS plays a crucial role in governing feeding and energy metabolism. In this context, the ARC serves as an important brain region that has been extensively studied over the last two decades [ 2 ]. In the ARC, AgRP/NPY neurons are responsible for sensing hunger signals (e.g., ghrelin), whereas pro-opiomelanocortin (POMC) neurons detect satiety cues. These two types of neurons play opposite roles in regulating feeding behavior and energy homeostasis and have become the focus of this research field [ 3 ]. The prominence of AgRP neurons in the regulation of feeding, body weight, and energy metabolism has made them among the most extensively researched neurons in the brain. In addition to the hypothalamus, various neuron types in the brainstem are crucial for the regulation of feeding and energy metabolism [ 4 ]. Recent studies have emphasized the contributions of catecholaminergic (CA) neurons and NPY neurons in the nucleus of the solitary tract (NTS) to promoting feeding and maintaining energy homeostasis [ 5 ]. Both the ARC and NTS are first-order brain areas that sense peripheral signals. Unlike ARC neurons, which detect nutrition-related signals (e.g., ghrelin and leptin) through the humoral pathway, NTS neurons detect peripheral signals through the vagal afferent. Additionally, the ventrolateral medulla (VLM) in the brainstem senses peripheral signals through the spinal nerves and plays important roles in the regulation of feeding behavior and energy metabolism [ 6 , 7 ]. Research on feeding-regulated neurons in the hypothalamus and brainstem, along with an exploration of their potential interactions, will offer valuable insights into the neuronal mechanisms governing energy balance and metabolic health. Owing to space limitations, this review will mainly delve into the following parts: (1) the historical route and major advancements of hypothalamic AgRP neurons in the regulation of feeding; (2) the ongoing progress in research surrounding AgRP neurons in the modulation of energy metabolism; (3) recent breakthroughs in understanding the role of the brainstem in governing feeding and energy metabolism; and (4) the challenges and prospects in the brain's regulation of feeding and energy metabolism. Progress of AgRP Neurons in Feeding Behavior Discovery of AgRP Neurons In the late 20th century, researchers discovered that mice carrying certain alleles of the Agouti coat color gene presented features such as yellow fur, obesity, and increased body length [ 8 , 9 ]. These phenotypes were primarily induced by the widespread expression of a chimeric transcript encoding a normal Agouti protein. Subsequent research isolated a gene encoding an Agouti-related protein, named AgRP [ 10 ]. Notably, AgRP was found to be predominantly expressed in the hypothalamus, as well as in the brainstem area postrema [ 11 ], adrenal glands [ 12 ], and pituitary gland [ 13 ]. The expression of AgRP is obviously higher in ob/ob obese mice than in wild-type (WT) mice, and the overexpression of the AgRP gene in mice has been correlated with the development of obesity [ 14 , 15 ]. Studies have focused on AgRP neurons because of their pivotal role in maintaining homeostasis [ 16 ]. AgRP neurons co-express AgRP and NPY [ 17 ] and use gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) as their neurotransmitter (Fig. 1 ) [ 18 , 19 ]. Functionally, AgRP neurons are activated during hunger and respond to signals from gastrointestinal hunger hormones [ 20 , 21 ]. Through the expression of receptors for critical hormones such as ghrelin, leptin, and insulin, these neurons possess the capacity to discern the body’s nutritional status cues [ 22 ]. Notably, AgRP neurons can be excited by ghrelin and inhibited by leptin and insulin [ 23 – 25 ]. Moreover, external environmental factors exert a considerable influence on the activity of AgRP neurons; for instance, cold environments have been shown to augment energy expenditure, activate AgRP neurons, and ultimately stimulate food intake [ 26 , 27 ]. Loss of Function and Gain of Function of Studies on AgRP Neurons Studies elucidating the loss of function and gain of function of AgRP neurons have provided invaluable insights into the central regulation of feeding behavior and energy metabolism. Both AgRP and NPY exert potent orexigenic effects, and the injection of these neuropeptides into the brain can induce feeding behavior directly [ 28 – 30 ]. Intriguingly, surprising findings were reported in NPY -/- knockout mice, AgRP -/- knockout mice, and NPY -/- :AgRP -/- double knockout mice, whose feeding patterns, body weights, and growth and development remain unaltered compared with those of WT mice [ 30 , 31 ]. This interesting observation emphasizes the existence of compensatory mechanisms in the absence of AgRP and NPY, thereby maintaining the energy balance between feeding and body weight. To avoid potential developmental compensatory effects, researchers designed a groundbreaking study to ablate AgRP neurons in adult mice. They targeted the expression of the human diphtheria toxin receptor (DTR) to the Agrp locus in embryonic stem cells and generated AgRP-DTR mice. Following intramuscular (IM) or intraperitoneal (IP) injections of diphtheria toxin (DT) into adult AgRP-DTR mice, AgRP neurons and their projections in downstream areas are ablated [ 32 , 33 ]. They reported that adult mice lacking AgRP neurons ceased feeding, rapidly lost body weight, and even succumbed to starvation. Given that AgRP-expressing cells exist only in the ARC, they concluded that AgRP neurons are mandatory for feeding, body weight maintenance, and growth in adult animals. This discovery has been widely acknowledged, echoed, and emphasized in the scientific literature and textbooks (Fig. 2 A) [ 34 ]. Fig. 2. With the development of neuroscience technologies, such as optogenetics and chemogenetics, researchers published two important studies in 2011 that marked another milestone in the understanding of AgRP neurons (Fig. 2 B) [ 35 ]. By selectively expressing channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2) or designer receptors exclusively activated by the designer drug (DREADDs) on AgRP neurons, these AgRP neurons are selectively activated and induce rapid and robust feeding behavior, culminating in binge eating [ 36 , 37 ]. This effect is universal in mouse models and occurs at any time and is unaffected by training, satiety, etc. After in-depth verification through optogenetic experiments, a significant correlation was found between the feeding response and the activation of AgRP neurons in a mouse model. Specifically, this correlation is manifested in factors such as stimulation frequency, laser power, and the number of AgRP neurons expressing ChR2 [ 37 , 38 ]. These findings confirm that AgRP neurons are key neurons in regulating and governing feeding behavior. At this point, it is generally believed that the activation of AgRP neurons promotes feeding, whereas the destruction or loss of AgRP neurons decreases appetite and stops feeding. In 2020, a research group first utilized chemogenetics to chronically activate AgRP neurons. Initially, the activation of AgRP neurons stimulates feeding behavior and weight gain, but this effect becomes transient over the long term [ 39 ]. Notably, the increase in daily food intake was observed only during the first week and quickly reverted to baseline compared with that in the control group. Moreover, the body weight returned to baseline levels following 60 days of chemogenetic activation of AgRP neurons. Conversely, another group adopted a different approach to increase AgRP neuron activity by expressing a bacterial sodium channel (NachBac). The long-term activation of AgRP neurons with NachBac resulted in a sustained increase in food intake and body weight, leading to severe obesity [ 15 ] (Fig. 2 C). These contrasting outcomes between the two studies are likely attributed to variances in experimental methodologies. Because the use of chemogenetics for prolonged activation of AgRP neurons may result in desensitization and subsequent loss of efficacy, overexpression of NachBac seems to provide a more natural mechanism for sustained activation of neurons, thereby increasing the robustness of its findings. These intriguing discoveries further confirm the pivotal role of AgRP neurons in the complex regulation of feeding behavior and body weight. The Effect of Feeding on AgRP Neuron Activity Because the activation of AgRP neurons can promote food intake, a long-held assumption was that AgRP neurons should be activated during eating. However, a surprising revelation surfaced in 2015 when researchers discovered an unexpected pattern: the activation of AgRP neurons did not surge at the onset or during meal consumption; rather, their activity was notably suppressed [ 40 – 42 ]. This unexpected observation contradicts the expected response, leading to a reassessment of the role of AgRP neurons in regulating feeding behavior. While hunger can elicit an increase in the activity of AgRP neurons, this new finding challenges the idea that enhanced neuronal activity is indispensable for the initiation and sustenance of feeding behavior. An intriguing hypothesis presumes that although AgRP neurons may be triggered by hunger, the sharp increase in their activity during feeding may be superfluous for the orchestration of feeding behavior. Notably, a subsequent study in 2020 introduced a novel approach involving the overexpression of the inward rectifier potassium channel Kir2.1 on AgRP neurons to chronically inhibit their activity. Surprisingly, the long-term suppression of AgRP neuron activity failed to evoke any obvious alterations in weight or feeding patterns in adult mice [ 15 ]. These results raised doubts regarding the necessity of AgRP neurons in maintaining feeding and weight. Selective Ablation of Adult AgRP Neurons To directly test the necessity of AgRP neurons, our laboratory, and collaborators selectively ablated AgRP neurons in adult AgRP-DTR mice via intracerebroventricular (i.c.v.) injection of DT. Unlike previous methodologies involving systemic DT injection, i.c.v. injection of DT selectively targeted AgRP neurons in the brain without affecting AgRP cells in peripheral organs such as the pituitary gland and adrenal gland [ 12 , 13 ]. In contrast to previous studies, ablation of AgRP neurons through low-dose DT injection did not impact body weight or ad libitum feeding in adult mice [ 43 ]. Interestingly, administering high doses of DT via the same method yielded outcomes similar to previous findings, including weight loss, decreased appetite, and even mortality in DTR and control mice. These results suggest that AgRP neurons are not essential for maintaining normal feeding and body weight homeostasis and that high doses of DT may induce nonspecific toxic effects. To avoid the nonspecific toxicity of DT, we selectively expressed Caspase-3 on AgRP neurons to induce neuronal apoptosis (Fig. 2 D) [ 43 ]. Similarly, using this method to ablate AgRP neurons did not result in decreased body weight or food intake in adult mice. Interestingly, while ablation of AgRP neurons had no effect on daily food intake or weight, it did impair fasting feeding (Fig. 2 D), suggesting that AgRP neurons may play a role in the response to environmental stress. By using two distinct methods to ablate AgRP neurons, our comprehensive study culminated in a crucial conclusion: AgRP neurons exert minimal influence on baseline feeding and body weight in environments abundant with food resources. However, their significant functions appear to be more pronounced in modulating feeding behavior and weight regulation under conditions of food deficiency or relative scarcity [ 12 ]. Interaction Between AgRP Neurons and Other Hypothalamic Neurons in the Regulation of Food Intake and Metabolism AgRP neurons and POMC neurons are two important neurons in the ARC that regulate feeding and energy balance [ 24 , 44 ]. AgRP neurons and POMC neurons have opposite effects on metabolic regulation [ 45 ]. AgRP neurons stimulate food intake and reduce energy expenditure, whereas POMC neurons decrease food intake and increase energy expenditure [ 3 ]. Orexigenic signals, such as ghrelin, increase the activation of AgRP neurons, whereas anorexigenic signals, such as leptin, insulin, and glucagon-like peptide (Glp1), activate POMC neurons and inhibit AgRP neurons [ 21 , 46 ]. The majority of AgRP neurons are GABAergic neurons and provide an inhibitory input to nearby POMC neurons. Therefore, leptin can also increase the excitability of POMC neurons by suppressing the inhibitory input from AgRP neurons [ 17 ]. There are other orexigenic neurons in the hypothalamus, such as somatostatin neurons in the tuberal nucleus (TN) [ 47 ]. There are mutual connections between ARC AgRP neurons and somatostatin neurons. Moreover, AgRP neurons and somatostatin neurons project to common downstream areas, such as the paraventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus (PVN), bed nucleus of the stria terminalis (BNST), and central nucleus of the amygdala (CeA). However, the functional interaction between these two types of orexigenic neurons remains unclear. Neural Circuit Mechanisms by Which AgRP Neurons Regulate Feeding AgRP neurons connect with various brain regions to regulate feeding behavior. These neural circuits coordinate with each other in metabolic homeostasis. AgRP neurons project widely to multiple brain regions, including the PVN, paraventricular nucleus of the thalamus (PVT), lateral hypothalamus (LH), ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH), dorsomedial hypothalamus (DMH), BNST, parabrachial nucleus (PBN), and other brain regions [ 21 , 38 ]. Moreover, the AgRP can also receive signals from diverse brain regions, such as the PVN, DMH, lateral septum (LS), PVT, and medial preoptic area (MPO) [ 48 ]. AgRP neurons receive signals from various populations of neurons in the PVN, including thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH) neurons and pituitary adenylate cyclase-activating polypeptide (PACAP) neurons, which play a role in fasting-induced feeding [ 49 ]. Furthermore, activation of the PBN produces significant anorexic effects. The PBN receives GABAergic inputs from AgRP neurons, and activation of the PBN by either inhibiting the release of GABA from AgRP neurons or blocking GABA receptors in the PBN leads to the suppression of feeding behaviors [ 50 ]. Recent studies have revealed that glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor (GLP1R) agonists can activate GLP1R neurons in the DMH, inhibiting AgRP neurons and promoting pre-ingestive satiation [ 51 ]. These findings emphasized the extensive circuit of AgRP neurons and their diverse physiological roles in regulating feeding behaviors. The interplay between AgRP neurons and various brain regions reveals a complex network related to feeding behavior and metabolic control. Progress of AgRP Neurons in Energy Metabolism Regulation AgRP Neurons Regulate Energy Expenditure, Autophagy, and Energy Mobilization The intricate role of AgRP neurons extends beyond the regulation of feeding behavior, including pivotal functions in energy expenditure, autophagy, and energy mobilization, which are crucial components of energy homeostasis. Starvation or food restriction typically correlates with decreased energy expenditure. Notably, i.c.v. administration of AgRP or NPY has been shown to reduce energy expenditure and attenuate sympathetic activity in brown adipose tissue (BAT), suggesting that AgRP neurons play a role in regulating energy expenditure [ 52 , 53 ]. Hunger-induced secretion of corticosterone has been demonstrated to be mediated by AgRP neurons, initiating hepatocyte autophagy, which is a crucial process in energy mobilization and maintenance [ 54 , 55 ]. The inhibition of AgRP neurons leads to a reduction in liver autophagy, while their activation can stimulate hepatic autophagy even in the absence of fasting [ 54 ]. This highlights the essential role of AgRP neurons in promoting liver autophagy during fasting, emphasizing their importance in energy mobilization. By inhibiting GABAergic inputs from the BNST to the PVN, AgRP neurons stimulate corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) neurons within the PVN, activating the hypothalamic‒pituitary‒adrenal (HPA) axis. This activation leads to the release of corticosterone, promoting energy storage [ 55 ]. These studies highlight the functions of AgRP neurons in energy expenditure, autophagy modulation, energy mobilization, and metabolic regulation. In addition to the effects of common macronutrients on AgRP neurons, micronutrients such as iron also play important roles in regulating energy metabolism through AgRP neurons. Iron overload has been linked to metabolic disorders [ 56 ]. Interestingly, high-fat diet-induced obesity increased iron levels in AgRP neurons. Pharmacological or genetic interventions targeting the iron content of AgRP neurons alleviate diet-induced obesity and related metabolic dysfunction [ 56 ]. These findings underscore the crucial interplay between AgRP neuronal metabolism and systemic energy metabolism. AgRP Neurons Influence Glucose Homeostasis Glucose is a primary energy source for mammals. AgRP neurons can sense, use, and regulate glucose [ 57 ]. An increase in insulin signaling in AgRP neurons changes feeding behavior and combats metabolic disorders [ 58 ]. The activation of AgRP neurons reduces insulin sensitivity and glucose tolerance by inhibiting glucose uptake in brown adipose tissues [ 59 , 60 ]. In addition, AgRP neurons project to several brain regions, such as the PVN, LH, and BNST, to regulate glucose metabolism [ 57 ]. Key Genes in AgRP Neurons Influencing Energy Metabolism AgRP neurons express several factors that influence energy metabolism and play indispensable roles in maintaining metabolic homeostasis. Several key genes and regulatory factors within AgRP neurons have been identified as critical determinants in regulating energy balance and metabolic function: MicroRNA-33 (miRNA-33): Selective knockout of miRNA-33 in AgRP neurons has been shown to perturb the expression of genes involved in mitochondrial biogenesis and fatty acid metabolism. Loss of miRNA-33 leads to increased food intake, obesity, and metabolic dysfunction, underscoring its pivotal role in energy regulation [ 61 ]. Targeting miRNA-33 in AgRP neurons represents a promising drug target for modulating appetite and metabolic-related diseases caused by excessive nutrient intake. Transcription factor 4 (ATF4): Specific knockout of ATF4 in AgRP neurons results in a reduction in food intake and weight loss in mice [ 62 ]. ATF4 is a promising therapeutic target for combating obesity and metabolic disorders. Forkhead box i2 (Foxi2): Identified as a novel nutrient sensor regulator. The overexpression of Foxi2 in AgRP neurons increased food intake and decreased energy expenditure, highlighting its regulatory role in energy metabolism through AgRP neurons [ 63 ]. β-arrestin-1 (barr1) is an important signaling factor in the peripheral regulation of glucose metabolism homeostasis. Recent studies have shown that selective knockout of the barr1 gene in AgRP neurons leads to impaired glucose tolerance and insulin sensitivity in high-fat-fed mice, whereas selective overexpression of the barr1 gene in AgRP neurons can prevent obesity-related metabolic phenotypes [ 64 ]. Carnitine palmitoyltransferase 1A (CPT1A): This key enzyme is instrumental in mitochondrial fatty acid oxidation and energy balance regulation. The specific knockout of CPT1A in AgRP neurons has been associated with reduced food intake in male mice and increased energy expenditure in female mice, revealing notable gender-specific effects on the regulatory mechanisms of CPT1A in AgRP neurons [ 65 ]. Leptin and leptin receptor (LepR): Peripheral leptin is secreted by adipose tissues. Leptin can suppress food intake and increase energy expenditure through AgRP neurons [ 66 ]. Mutant LepR in AgRP neurons induces obesity and impairs glucose metabolism [ 67 ]. G-protein–coupled receptor 17 (Gpr17): Gpr17 was identified as a dual uracil nucleotide/cysteinyl‐leukotriene receptor [ 68 ]. Increasing the expression of Gpr17 in AgRP neurons induces food intake [ 69 ]. Ablation of Gpr17 in AgRP neurons inhibits feeding behavior and increases energy expenditure [ 70 ]. Gpr17 antagonists may be promising drugs for preventing obesity. NPY: NPY is well known for its ability to regulate food intake and metabolism. Recent studies have shown that NPY is different from AgRP. AgRP neurons secrete three neurotransmitters: AgRP, NPY, and GABA (Fig. 1 ) [ 71 , 72 ]. NPY in AgRP neurons regulates rapid food intake and glucose homeostasis [ 73 ]. In addition, NPY has a long-lasting effect on postsynaptic targets of AgRP neurons and sustains hunger through downstream brain regions [ 74 ]. TCPTP: TCPTP (encoded by ptpn2 ) is well known to regulate feeding and glucose metabolism. Feeding rhythms affect the expression of TCPTP [ 75 ]. Fasting increases the expression of TCPTP, whereas refeeding suppresses the expression of TCPTP. TCPTP can suppress the activation of insulin receptor (IR) signaling in AgRP neurons, whereas ablation of TCPTP in AgRP neurons can improve the sensitivity of insulin [ 76 ]. Understanding the functions of these factors and genes in AgRP neurons may offer promising targets for treating obesity and metabolic disorders. The Role of the Brainstem in the Regulation of Feeding and Energy Metabolism The NTS Regulates Feeding Behavior The NTS, a primary region for bidirectional communication between the brain and peripheral organs, acts as a key hub for transferring chemical and mechanical signals from peripheral organs to the brain via vagus afferents [ 77 , 78 ]. Recent research has underscored the vital importance of this brainstem region in controlling both feeding behavior and energy metabolism [ 79 ]. The NTS consists of various populations of neurons, including POMC neurons [ 80 ], glucagon-like peptide 1 (GCG) neurons [ 81 ], cholecystokinin (CCK) neurons [ 82 ], LepR neurons [ 83 ], calcitonin receptor (Calcr) neurons [ 84 ], prolactin-releasing hormone (Prlh) neurons [ 85 ], 5-hydroxytryptamine 2C receptors (5-HT2C) [ 86 ], and preprotachykinin 1 (Tac1) [ 87 ]. The majority of these neurons are anorexigenic neurons that induce satiety and suppress food intake (Fig. 3 ) [ 4 , 88 ]. Moreover, multiple vagus sensory neurons exhibit anorexigenic properties. For example, GLP1R neurons in the nodose ganglion respond to the stomach and intestinal expansion, whereas neurons expressing G protein-coupled receptor 65 (GPR65) innervate the intestinal villi and detect intestinal nutrient levels [ 89 ], with both types of neurons suppressing feeding. Another study revealed that the nodose ganglion-NTS-PBN-substantia nigra (SNc) pathway mediates satiety-induced reward signals [ 90 ]. Signals of stretching from the intestines can be relayed to the brain via the vagus nerve, signaling cessation of eating in hungry mice [ 91 ]. In general, the NTS is recognized as a satiety center that receives satiety signals from the gastrointestinal tract via vagal afferents. Fig. 3. Neuronal subsets in the NTS and VLM. Both the NTS and VLM contain orexigenic CA and NPY neurons, whereas many other neuronal subsets in the NTS are anorexigenic. Recently, studies have revealed the orexigenic effect of NTS CA neurons and their responsiveness to hunger signals via vagal afferents [ 5 , 92 ]. Activation of a subset of NTS CA neurons that co-express NPY promotes feeding [ 5 ]. Furthermore, NTS CA neurons are implicated in transmitting nutrient-related signals to AgRP neurons, suggesting an interplay between these two populations of orexigenic neurons [ 92 ]. The VLM in the Brainstem The VLM serves as another crucial link between the brain and body, governing the autonomic nervous system and the neuroendocrine system. Within the VLM, CA neurons act as premotor neurons of the sympathetic system and respond to diverse stresses, including hypoxia, hypotension, glucose deprivation, and fasting. These VLM CA neurons are likened to the body’s “emergency medical technicians” because of their critical ability to handle physiological and metabolic challenges during stress [ 93 ]. VLM CA neurons can respond to nutrient-related stress, such as glucose deficits caused by 2-deoxy-D-glucose (2DG) [ 94 ]. These neurons play crucial roles in initiating counterregulatory responses to glucose deficits, which are essential for maintaining metabolic homeostasis during stress [ 6 ]. The activation of VLM CA neurons stimulates food intake [ 95 ] and increases blood glucose levels [ 7 ]. Furthermore, these neurons integrate various physical and psychological stressors from stress-related brain regions, ultimately inducing hyperglycemia through descending projections to the spinal cord [ 7 ]. Although VLM CA neurons are activated in response to fasting, they are not considered essential for the fasting-refeeding response. Nevertheless, their activation plays a role in aspects of energy conservation during fasting by dampening excessive inflammatory responses such as autoimmune diseases [ 96 ]. Similar to AgRP neurons, direct activation of VLM CA neurons stimulates the HPA axis, resulting in the release of glucocorticoids, thereby influencing both feeding behavior and energy metabolism [ 97 ]. A subset of VLM CA neurons also co-express NPY and project to the hypothalamus [ 98 ]. Studies have indicated that NPY released from VLM CA neurons plays a crucial role in glucosidic feeding responses. Simultaneous inhibition of dopamine beta-hydroxylase (DBH), a key CA synthetic enzyme, and NPY hampers glucosidic feeding [ 99 ]. Lesions targeted to NPY receptor-expressing neurons in the hypothalamus impair glucosidic feeding, underscoring the importance of NPY in modulating feeding behavior and metabolic responses orchestrated by VLM CA neurons [ 100 ]. The integrated functions of VLM CA neurons in responding to stressors, regulating feeding behavior, and modulating energy metabolism highlight their pivotal role in adapting to physiological challenges and maintaining metabolic homeostasis. Challenges and Opportunities On the basis of the above discussions, the ARC in the hypothalamus, as well as the NTS and VLM in the brainstem, play important roles in regulating feeding and energy metabolism. The intricate interactions among these regions represent a complex neural network that governs feeding behavior and energy metabolism. While significant progress has been made in understanding the roles of these brain regions and neurons, there are still several critical questions that need further exploration. The challenges and opportunities in deciphering these neural circuits include the following (Fig. 4 ): Coordination and interaction among different populations of NPY neurons: NPY neurons in the ARC, NTS, and VLM mediate various responses to hunger stimuli [ 101 ]. Exploring how these ARC and brainstem NPY neurons coordinate and interact with one another to regulate feeding and energy metabolism, despite physical distance and distinct connectivity, is crucial. Understanding the reciprocity of these orexigenic neurons and their compensatory mechanisms is vital for unraveling the neural regulation of feeding and metabolism. Functional differences between the hypothalamus and brainstem: Long-term activation of AgRP neurons in the hypothalamus is associated with obesity, suggesting that the hypothalamus is vital for long-term energy homeostasis. In contrast, the contributions of brainstem NPY and CA neurons to long-term energy regulation remain unclear and need further investigation to delineate their distinct roles in maintaining metabolic homeostasis. Interactions between feeding and immune function: Feeding behavior significantly influences various physiological functions, including immune responses. Recent studies have shown that moderate hunger can help inhibit inflammatory responses and delay the onset of several autoimmune diseases. Previous studies have highlighted the role of moderate hunger in suppressing inflammatory responses and delaying the onset of autoimmune diseases. Research has indicated that VLM CA neurons play a role in alleviating autoimmunity under moderate hunger, suggesting that feeding-related neural circuits are involved in modulating immune function [ 94 ]. Similarly, ARC AgRP neurons reduce the levels of inflammatory cytokines induced by lipopolysaccharide [ 102 , 103 ] and alleviate inflammatory pain [ 104 ]. Further exploration of the underlying neural circuits, cellular interactions, and molecular mechanisms is necessary to fully understand how feeding-related neural circuits regulate immune function and contribute to immune diseases. In conclusion, further exploration of the neurons and neural circuits implicated in the regulation of feeding and energy metabolism holds significant promise for elucidating central regulatory mechanisms for the treatment and prevention of metabolic and immune-related diseases. Continued research in this area is anticipated to unveil novel targets and therapeutic approaches to address the intricate interplay between neural regulation, metabolic health, and immune function.
