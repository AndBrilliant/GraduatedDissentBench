Introduction Colorectal cancer and prostate cancer are among the most prevalent cancers in the United States, with 1.3 million people living with colorectal cancer and 3.1 million people living with prostate cancer [ 1 ]. Although improvements in survival have occurred for both cancer types, colorectal and prostate cancer survivors endure a number of disease- and treatment-related morbidities, including sexual dysfunction and incontinence following surgery and radiation; fatigue, osteopenia, and cardiovascular disease; and an overall reduced quality of life (QOL) [ 2 – 6 ]. The promotion of healthy behaviors that reduce the adverse effects of treatment, improve QOL, and potentially lengthen survival is a key component of cancer survivorship care [ 7 ]. Multiple observational studies and randomized controlled trials (RCTs) have reported that exercise and healthy diet may be beneficial for both cancer prognosis and improvement in survivorship [ 8 – 13 ]. A meta-analysis of six prospective observational studies reported that high versus low physical activity after diagnosis was associated with a 42% lower risk of total mortality and a 39% lower risk of colorectal cancer-specific mortality [ 9 ]. Similar strong associations with physical activity have been reported for total and prostate cancer specific-mortality in men with prostate cancer [ 14 ]. Additionally, aerobic exercise may reduce sexual dysfunction following radical prostatectomy [ 10 ], and exercise alone, or in combination with healthy dietary modifications, may reduce symptoms of metabolic syndrome and osteopenia from androgen deprivation therapy [ 11 , 12 ]. Many national organization such as the American Cancer Society, The American College of Sports Medicine, and the American Institute of Cancer Research/ World Cancer Research Fund have acknowledged the important role that healthy behaviors play in improving cancer survivorship and include them in recommendations for both colorectal and prostate cancer survivorship care [ 5 , 6 ]. Yet, implementing healthy behaviors can be challenging for cancer survivors for multiple age- or disease-related (e.g., fatigue, functional disability, concerns about stoma), personal (e.g., knowledge, motivation, time, social support related to cultural/familial norms), and environmental (e.g., neighborhood safety, access to exercise facilities) reasons [ 15 – 18 ]. These perceived barriers prevent individuals from engaging in regular exercise or maintaining a healthy diet. In this work, we sought to explore perceived barriers to engaging in physical activity and/or eating a healthy diet, and whether barriers or confidence change following participation in 12-week technology-administered information interventions. In particular, we conducted secondary analyses of two separate studies with distinct treatment arms. Smart Pace (SP) and Prostate 8 (P8) were pilot remote-based randomized controlled trials (RCTs) among colorectal and prostate cancer survivors, respectively, that investigated the feasibility and acceptability of text messaging and wearable fitness monitors for promoting and sustaining exercise behaviors. The P8 study also included online behavior implementation resources and targeted a healthy diet in addition to exercise [ 19 , 20 ]. In SP, participants in the intervention arm wore the Fitbits for 88% of the 12-week period and responded to 74% of the 46 text messages that asked for a reply. In P8, intervention arm participants wore the Fitbits for 98% of the days in the 12-week intervention phase and responded to a median of 71% of the 60 text messages that asked for a response. In this paper, we described perceived barriers to and confidence in implementing healthy behaviors among colorectal and prostate cancer survivors using patient-reported data from SP and P8, and incorporating constructs and strategies based on the Theory of Planned Behavior [ 21 ]. We further explored how perceived barriers to and confidence in implementing healthy behaviors were modified during the intervention periods. Methods Participant recruitment - SP Data were collected from 42 colorectal cancer survivors participating in SP ( NCT02966054 ), the details of which have been described previously [ 19 ]. In brief, SP was a 12-week pilot RCT in people with colorectal cancer who had completed treatment with curative intent to determine the feasibility of an intervention using a Fitbit and daily text messages. Participants had to be considered disease-free at enrollment (based on their last exam found in record review; no clinical exams were required). Those who were already very active at baseline (defined as engaging in exercise for at least 30 minutes a day, for 5 or more days a week) were excluded from enrollment. Other primary reasons for ineligibility included non-English speaking status and medical contraindication to exercise. Participants were not screened for previous use, knowledge of, or expectation of personal fitness trackers. Participants in the intervention arm received print informational materials on exercise, were provided with a Fitbit to monitor activity levels, and received daily supportive text messages consisting of exercise tips, encouraging notes, and reminders to complete their exercise goal for the day. Participants in the control arm received the print materials and continued with usual care; they were provided with a Fitbit after completion of the 12-week study assessments. Follow-up was 95% complete in the intervention arm and 90% complete in the control arm. This study obtained ethics approval at the University of California, San Francisco (UCSF). Participant recruitment - P8 Data were collected from 76 prostate cancer survivors participating in P8 ( NCT02470936 ), the details of which have been described previously [ 20 ]. In brief, P8 was a 12-week pilot RCT of participants with clinical stage T1-T3a prostate cancer who chose active surveillance or radical prostatectomy as treatment. Eligibility criteria included having internet access, no medical contraindications to aerobic exercise, and engaging in 4 or fewer of the 8 targeted lifestyle behaviors (assessed via brief questionnaires at screening). Questionnaires evaluated whether a potential participant was a current smoker, their level of light, moderate, and/or strenuous activity with examples included (ranging from none or &lt;20 minutes per week to 7+ hours a week), their consumption of food items (using select items taken from a validated food frequency questionnaire, where participants reported their average intake of a standard portion of each food item, ranging from never or &lt;1 serving per month to 6+ servings per day), and supplement use (if yes, what reason, e.g., prostate health, doctor recommended, or other reason specified by participant). Participants in the intervention group received these eight evidence-based recommendations on exercise, nutrition, and smoking cessation for improving prostate cancer outcomes. Intervention participants were given access to a website with informational materials on how to implement the recommendations and received regular text messages to support behavior implementation. They were also provided with a Fitbit to monitor activity levels. As in SP, participants were not screened for previous use of personal fitness trackers. The control group continued with usual care; however, the website materials were available to the control arm after the 12-week study ended. Follow-up was 94% complete in the intervention arm and 84% in the control arm at 12 weeks (main study period). A 12-month follow-up questionnaire was added as a protocol amendment near the end of the enrollment period, with 68% and 62% completing the survey in the intervention and control arms, respectively. This study obtained ethics approval at UCSF. Data collection Demographic variables such as age, race, education level, and marital status were collected at enrollment in both studies using online surveys in Research Electronic Data Capture (REDCap) [ 22 ]. Self-reported exercise (both studies) and dietary habits (P8 only) were collected online using validated surveys [ 23 , 24 ]. Self-reported activities with a metabolic equivalent task (MET) value ≥6 were considered vigorous [ 25 ]. Objective exercise measurements were collected using 7-days of the ActiGraph GTX3+ accelerometer worn around the waist at enrollment and 12 weeks in both studies [ 26 , 27 ]. Participants received surveys inquiring about their perceptions regarding implementation of exercise (both studies) and dietary (P8 only) habits in two domains: barriers to implementation and confidence in ability to implement behaviors. These domains were further divided into 17 subdomains ( Supplementary Table 1 ). Barriers were assessed on a 5-point scale (modified from scales developed by UCSD) [ 28 ]; participants were considered to have a specific barrier if they responded “sometimes,” “often,” or “very often” to a question about the barrier. Confidence was also assessed on a 5-point scale; participants were considered to have low confidence if they responded “maybe I can” or “I know I cannot” to a question. Data analysis All analyses were conducted separately for colorectal cancer survivors in SP and prostate cancer survivors in P8. Demographic and clinical characteristics, as well as exercise and dietary habits at enrollment, were described using means with standard deviations (SDs) for parametric continuous variables, medians with interquartile ranges (IQRs) for non-parametric continuous variables, and frequency with percentage for categorical variables. The 5-point scales for the barriers and confidence measures were modified so that a score of 1 indicated the lowest confidence in or greatest barrier to implementation and a score of 5 indicated the greatest confidence in or lowest barrier to implementation. Scores for barriers to and confidence in behavior implementation as well as absolute change in scores from enrollment to follow-up (12 weeks for SP, 12 and 52 weeks for P8) were described using means with SD. A positive change indicated an increased confidence or reduced barrier to implementation. and a negative change indicated a reduced confidence or increased barrier to implementation. The difference in changes from enrollment to follow-up between groups was described using means and 95% confidence intervals (CI). A positive number indicated greater positive change or smaller negative change from enrollment to 12 weeks among the intervention group than among the control group, and a negative number indicated a smaller positive change or greater negative change from enrollment to 12 weeks among the intervention group compared to the control group. We also conducted analyses stratified by body mass index (BMI) (&lt; or ≥25 kg/m2), marital status (married versus unmarried), and age (&lt; or ≥65 years) and explored whether the behavioral changes observed were correlated with participants’ initial responses to perceived barriers. Consistent with the Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials (CONSORT) guideline for randomized pilot and feasibility trials, we did not conduct statistical tests [ 29 ]. Results Participant characteristics and health behaviors at enrollment 42 participants were randomized in SP and 76 participants were randomized in P8. Sociodemographic, clinical, exercise, and diet characteristics at enrollment of the groups are shown in Table 1 . The majority of participants in SP and P8 identified as White (74% and 80%, respectively), were college educated (93% and 74%, respectively), and were overweight (BMI 27.5 and 28.5 kg/m2, respectively). Median time since diagnosis was 1 year [IQR 0,8] among SP participants and 2 years [IQR 1,4] among P8. The majority of participants in SP had regional or advanced disease (60% stage III or greater), whereas most participants in P8 had localized disease (93% stage II or less). Mean steps per day (as reported via accelerometer) among SP and P8 was 9,758 [IQR 7,210, 14,196] and 6,904 [IQR 5,046, 9,205], respectively; SP participants also self-reported 283.3 minutes per week of exercise (IQR 199.8, 364.6) compared to 329.0 minutes per week (IQR 174.4, 475.9) of P8 participants. Although participants in both groups had varied baseline characteristics, with notable difference in staging (per the eligibility criteria), SP participants—despite having more advanced disease stage—had a slightly lower BMI, were younger, and were more active in comparison to P8 participants. Exercise perceptions at enrollment and changes over time among prostate cancer survivors Exercise perception summary scores at enrollment, mean change, and difference in means between groups among prostate cancer survivors are presented in Table 3b . Scores at enrollment among both groups skewed high with some room for improvement in the barriers subdomain excuses (intervention group: enrollment mean=3.5, SD=0.7; control group: enrollment mean=3.5, SD=0.6). Minimal change was observed from enrollment to 12 weeks in all categories. A small 52-week treatment difference in means between groups was observed in the confidence subdomain resisting relapse (−0.68, 95% CI −1.29, −0.07), where the mean difference in confidence was lower among the intervention group than among the control group (i.e., the intervention group had less confidence resisting relapse). When changes in exercise perception summary scores were evaluated by categories of BMI, a decrease was observed among healthy weight participants (BMI &lt;25 kg/m2) in the exercise confidence domain (−0.45, 95% CI −0.84, −0.6) as well as the confidence subdomains making time (−0.51, 95% CI −0.96, −0.05) and resisting relapse (−0.41, 95% CI −0.80, −0.02). Exercise perceptions did not appear to change meaningfully during the intervention among prostate cancer survivors with a higher BMI. Differences were small in all categories when stratified by marital status or age (data not shown). Diet perceptions at enrollment and changes over time among prostate cancer survivors Diet perception summary scores by study group at enrollment, 12 weeks, and 52 weeks among prostate cancer survivors are presented in Table 3c . For both groups, mean scores skewed high at enrollment with minimal change over the study period for most categories. At 12 weeks, a reduction in the confidence subdomain portions was observed in the control group (−0.4 SD 0.8). Additionally, at 52 weeks, there was a reduction in the confidence subdomain avoiding red meat among the control group (−0.4 SD 0.8). Participants’ perceived barriers at enrollment and subsequent behavioral changes Table 4 displays Spearman correlation coefficients between initial perceived barriers to healthy exercise and eating habits and changes in average moderate-to-vigorous physical activity (MVPA) (both studies) and diet changes (P8 only). For SP, the overall barriers score and the functional/psychological disability barrier category (higher scores = less barriers) were positively correlated (0.49) with change in average minutes of MVPA. In other words, individuals who had fewer barriers—in particular, fewer functional/psychological disability barriers—at enrollment, were more likely to increase their activity during the intervention. In contrast, within the sample of P8 participants, scores for exercise barriers ( overall barriers and categories of aversiveness, functional/ psychological disability, and excuses ) at enrollment were moderate-to-strongly negatively correlated (−0.42 to −0.53) with change in physical activity. So, among men with prostate cancer, having fewer barriers was associated with less physical activity change. For diet among prostate cancer survivors, higher scores (fewest barriers) overall , and within categories of functional/psychological disability and inconveniences , were moderately-to-strongly positively correlated with positive changes in dietary habits (0.43 to 0.5). There were multiple perceived barriers to behavior change at enrollment among colorectal and prostate cancer survivors. Over a third of colorectal cancer survivors expressed concerns regarding a lack of will-power or energy . A reason for lack of energy may be a result of physical weakness directly due to cancer treatment (e.g., pain, weakness, etc.); lack of will-power likely stems from the emotional distress that patients experience from their diagnosis and treatment: patients may feel anxious and/or overwhelmed, making it challenging to focus on making changes their established habits and routines and adopting new behaviors. Prior literature has also found that participants commonly report lack of motivation as the main barrier to physical activity and dietary change in cancer survivors [ 17 , 30 , 31 ]. Fatigue has also been noted as a significant barrier to exercise [ 15 , 16 , 32 – 34 ]. Lynch et al. reported that perceived barriers among 538 colorectal cancer survivors included not feeling physically well enough (45%), fatigue (41%), and a lack of exercise enjoyment or interest (26%) [ 15 ]. Work demands were also a common concern among colorectal cancer survivors (24%) in our study and a less common concern among prostate cancer survivors (12%), reflecting the fact that a greater proportion of the colorectal cancer survivors were employed full-time compared to the prostate cancer survivors (62% versus 34%, respectively). Work and/or a lack of time has been a commonly reported barrier in previous studies among patients with cancer [ 15 – 17 , 35 ]. Additionally, over a quarter of P8 participants reported a lack of knowledge regarding nutrition that benefits prostate cancer outcomes at enrollment. There is limited but growing literature on the benefits of diet after diagnosis of prostate cancer [ 36 , 37 ], and limited information in the public domain regarding diet and exercise guidance for individuals with specific cancers [ 38 , 39 ], which can make it difficult for survivors to understand the importance of physical exercise and healthy eating for their overall and cancer-specific health, as well as actively prioritizing their commitment to these changes. These data emphasize the importance of utilizing up-to-date and credible online resources (such as from the American Cancer Society, American Institute for Cancer Research, World Cancer Research Fund, Prostate Cancer Foundation, and University of California-San Francisco) [ 40 – 44 ] and discussing lifestyle modifications with patients during treatment and survivorship care. Among men with prostate cancer, we observed an increase in exercise barriers over time among leaner men relative to among overweight/obese men. This was an unexpected finding and may be due to chance; we did not observe this difference among people with colorectal cancer. The 12-week interventions for both trials did not meaningfully change exercise perceptions. Other behavior implementation interventions have reported similar results. For example, the FRESH START intervention trial was an RCT among patients with breast and prostate cancer. Informed by Social Cognitive Theory, the intervention group received individually tailored print materials on exercise and dietary modification for 10 months. Exercise levels did not vary by group assignment at 2-year follow up, nor did self-efficacy or barriers to exercise at 1-year follow up. However, the authors reported that, among individuals, exercise frequency at 2-year follow up was positively correlated with change in self-efficacy between enrollment and 1-year follow up, indicating that changing exercise habits may require long-term patient engagement [ 45 ]. Notably, the FRESH START intervention trial, which used an alternate theory of health-related decision-making, also reported improvements in self-efficacy for dietary changes, such as increasing plant-based foods and reducing fat intake at 1-year follow up [ 45 ]. In both studies, there was some evidence that enrollment barriers were associated with a change in diet and/or exercise within the intervention arms. Higher scores (i.e., lower barriers) of functional/psychological disability in the SP group demonstrated moderately strong positive correlation (0.49) with change in physical activity. This result indicated that participants who experienced fewer barriers at enrollment were more likely to increase their physical activity during the intervention. In contrast, lower exercise enrollment barriers ( overall barriers, aversiveness, functional/psychological disability , and excuses ) in the P8 group were moderate-to-strongly negatively associated with a change in physical activity from enrollment to 12-weeks. This finding suggested that fewer barriers were associated with less change in physical activity. This discrepancy may be due to a difference in exercise standards between studies: SP promoted moderate physical activity, whereas P8 focused on moderate-to-vigorous activity. There may also be a need for a more hands on approach and/or possibly a sequential intervening approach: SP participants may have experienced better outcomes in exercise as a result of focusing on exercise as the sole intervention, whereas the P8 group participated concurrently in both diet and exercise interventions. Intervening at the same time may be detrimental to people with high barriers due to feeling more overwhelmed and/or incapable of overcoming diet and exercise barriers simultaneously. Interestingly, in the same P8 group, those who had encountered fewer diet barriers at enrollment ( overall barriers, functional/psychological disability , and inconvenience ) experienced a moderate-to-high degree of correlation of positive change (&gt;0.4) in their P8 food score, indicating that the intervention may have been useful in helping participants adhere to food recommendations and guidelines provided by the study. At 52-week follow up, the differences between the intervention and control groups in P8 were minimal; this could be due the loss of supportive resources present during the trial for the intervention group, coupled with the control group receiving the P8 materials after the 12-week trial ended. It is also important to note the subset of the P8 group (16 participants—7 in intervention, 9 in control—lost to follow up) that had completed the 52-week survey may not be representative of the randomized population. Future research should focus on assisting participants’ transition to self-management in interventions and in sustaining these behaviors beyond study completion. Clinical implications Short and long-term barriers that cancer survivors face exemplify the difficulty of adopting behavior changes and emphasize the need to include exercise and dietary counseling and monitoring as a component of long-term cancer survivorship care. Support from mental health professionals or support groups for cancer survivors may help manage anxiety related to cancer treatment; connecting with others having similar experiences may also be of benefit in sharing coping strategies. Clinician involvement could be an effective means of improving health behaviors among patients with cancer [ 16 , 46 ]. Prior studies have reported that a lack of discussion with their provider regarding the benefits of exercise was a barrier to behavior implementation [ 15 , 16 , 35 , 47 , 48 ]. Speaking with healthcare specialists may provide guidance on behavioral changes; for example, physical therapists or exercise physiologists/trainers and/ or registered cancer dietitians may work with survivors to identify potential behaviors for behavior change, help set specific goals, develop individualized exercise and eating plans suited for their lifestyle during and post treatment, and track their progress over time. Starting with small and manageable changes may help in establishing new habits over time, aiding patients in feeling more motivated as they reach milestones in their progress. These different approaches may empower cancer survivors in increasing their confidence and overcoming the barriers they face. Study limitations We acknowledge that our study had limitations. First, in both SP and P8, the majority of participants identified as white and were college-educated, and participants of both trials were also physically active at enrollment. These characteristics may limit the generalizability of the findings. Second, SP only consisted of 12-week follow up, limiting the ability to ascertain long-term changes in perceptions from the intervention. P8 had a longer 52-week follow up; however, only a subset of the study population completed surveys at this time point. Third, the questionnaires in SP and P8 may not have comprehensively assessed all possible perceptions regarding behavioral change. A mixed methods approach in future studies that includes qualitative assessments would be helpful to more deeply understand participants’ barriers and confidences for health behavior change, and how interventions impact these constructs. Lastly, the study included two pilot studies and as such had a small sample size (n=42 for SP and n=76 for P8). Thus, these studies were not powered for formal analyses on perceived barriers or confidence levels. However, these data are informative for designing future behavioral interventions for cancer survivors. Conclusions There are multiple barriers to healthy behavior implementation among colorectal and prostate cancer survivors related to motivation (e.g., lacking discipline or willpower), time, social support, and lack of knowledge. This work found a negligible response of these perceived barriers to technologically administered interventions. There was some suggestion that long-term perceptions of barriers or confidence may worsen among those provided with short-term healthy behavior implementation resources, indicating the need for sustainable interventions that adjust to the individual’s needs over time.
